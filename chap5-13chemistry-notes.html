Chapter 5: Bohr's Atomic Theory & Postulates

Background

  • Niels Bohr (1913) combined Rutherford's nuclear model with Planck's quantum theory to fix the stability and line spectra problems.
  • Applied specifically and successfully to the hydrogen atom (single electron system).

Postulates of Bohr's Theory

  • Postulate 1 (Quantized Orbits): Electrons revolve around the nucleus only in certain fixed circular paths called orbits/shells/energy levels, designated by principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3...
  • Postulate 2 (Angular Momentum Quantization): The angular momentum of an electron in an orbit is quantized and is an integral multiple of h/2π: mvr = nh/2π
  • Postulate 3 (Stationary States): As long as an electron remains in a particular orbit, it does not radiate energy — these are called stationary/ground states. This directly solves Rutherford's stability defect.
  • Postulate 4 (Energy Absorption/Emission): An electron can jump from a lower energy orbit to a higher one by absorbing energy, and from higher to lower by emitting energy as a photon, where: ΔE = E₂ − E₁ = hν

Key Formulas (for Hydrogen-like species)

  • Radius of nth orbit: rₙ = (0.529 × n²)/Z Å
  • Energy of nth orbit: Eₙ = −(13.6 × Z²)/n² eV
  • Velocity of electron: vₙ = (2.18 × 10⁶ × Z)/n m/s
  • For H atom (Z=1), ground state (n=1): radius = 0.529 Å, energy = −13.6 eV

Explanation of Hydrogen Line Spectrum

  • Lyman series — electron falls to n=1 (UV region)
  • Balmer series — electron falls to n=2 (Visible region)
  • Paschen series — electron falls to n=3 (Infrared region)
  • Each series arises because transitions to the same final level produce a group of related spectral lines, explaining the discrete/line nature of the spectrum.

Limitations of Bohr's Theory

  • Could not explain spectra of multi-electron atoms (only worked for H and hydrogen-like ions: He⁺, Li²⁺).
  • Could not explain the splitting of spectral lines in magnetic field (Zeeman effect) or electric field (Stark effect).
  • Violated Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle — it assumes we can know both the exact position (fixed orbit) and exact velocity/momentum of an electron simultaneously.
  • Did not consider the wave nature of electrons (de Broglie concept, came later).
  • Circular orbits could not explain fine structure of spectral lines (later explained by elliptical orbits — Sommerfeld).

⚡ Quick Revision

  • Postulate that fixes Rutherford's defect: Postulate 3 (stationary states — no radiation while in an orbit).
  • mvr = nh/2π → memorize this; frequently tested directly.
  • Eₙ = −13.6/n² eV (for H) — ground state is most negative (most stable); energy increases (becomes less negative) as n increases.
  • Series order by energy/frequency: Lyman > Balmer > Paschen (Lyman has highest energy, shortest wavelength).
  • Top failure reason: Bohr's model fails for multi-electron atoms — most commonly asked limitation in AFNS-level MCQs.

Stability Unstable (electron spirals in) Stable (stationary states)

Chapter 6: Planck's Quantum Theory

Background — Black Body Radiation Problem

  • Classical physics ("wave theory of light") could not explain the energy distribution of radiation emitted by a heated black body (predicted infinite energy at short wavelengths — the "ultraviolet catastrophe").
  • Max Planck (1900) proposed a revolutionary solution to resolve this.

Planck's Quantum Theory (Postulates)

  • Energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously — it is emitted/absorbed in small discrete packets called quanta (for light, called photons).
  • The energy of one quantum is directly proportional to the frequency of radiation: E = hν, where h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s.
  • The total energy emitted/absorbed by a body is always a whole-number multiple of a quantum: E = nhν (n = 1, 2, 3...)
  • Since E = hν and c = νλ, we get: E = hc/λ — energy is inversely proportional to wavelength.

Significance & Applications

  • Successfully explained the black body radiation curve (resolved the ultraviolet catastrophe).
  • Provided the theoretical foundation for Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect (1905) — light must have particle-like (photon) character.
  • Became the foundation of Bohr's atomic model — Bohr used E = hν directly in his 4th postulate (ΔE = hν for electron transitions).
  • Marked the birth of Quantum Mechanics as a field.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • E = hν = hc/λ — the most repeated formula across the entire syllabus; appears in numericals for Ch.6, 7, and 8.
  • Energy and wavelength are inversely proportional; energy and frequency are directly proportional. A classic trick question reverses these.
  • Planck's constant h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s — memorize the value and units.
  • Planck's theory → basis for Einstein's photoelectric effect → basis for Bohr's postulate 4. This chain is a favorite "which came first" conceptual MCQ.

Chapter 7: X-Rays & Atomic Number (Moseley's Law)

Discovery & Production of X-Rays

  • Discovered by Wilhelm Roentgen (1895) — accidentally, while working with a cathode ray (discharge) tube.
  • Produced when high-speed cathode rays (electrons) strike a heavy metal target (e.g., tungsten or molybdenum) inside an evacuated tube under high voltage.
  • X-rays are electromagnetic radiation of very short wavelength (~0.01 to 10 nm) — much shorter than visible light, hence very high energy and penetrating power.
  • Not deflected by electric or magnetic fields (uncharged, unlike cathode/canal rays).

Moseley's Law (1913)

  • Henry Moseley studied the X-ray spectra of various elements and found a relationship between the frequency of emitted X-rays and the atomic number (Z) of the target element — not its atomic mass.
  • Mathematical form: √ν = a(Z − b), where 'a' and 'b' are constants (b is a screening constant, ≈1).
  • This means √ν is directly proportional to (Z − b) — a plot of √ν vs Z gives a straight line.
  • Significance: Moseley's law established that atomic number (Z), not atomic mass, is the fundamental property that determines an element's properties and its correct position in the periodic table.
  • This led to the correction of the periodic table — some elements were re-ordered based on Z instead of atomic mass (resolved anomalies like Co/Ni, Ar/K placements).
  • Also helped predict the existence of then-undiscovered elements by identifying gaps in atomic numbers.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • Moseley's Law formula: √ν = a(Z − b) — directly examinable.
  • Biggest significance: proved atomic number (not atomic mass) is the basis of periodic classification — this connects directly to Chapter 10 (Modern Periodic Table).
  • X-rays are produced by decelerating fast electrons hitting a metal target — link back to "cathode rays produce X-rays" from Ch.2.
  • Trap: X-rays are NOT charged particles (unlike cathode/canal rays) — they are electromagnetic waves, hence not deflected by fields.

Chapter 8: Quantum Numbers (Principal, Azimuthal, Magnetic, Spin)

1. Principal Quantum Number (n)

  • Denotes the main energy level/shell in which an electron is located.
  • Values: n = 1, 2, 3, 4... (also denoted K, L, M, N shells).
  • Determines the size of the orbital and most of the energy of the electron — larger n = larger orbital = higher energy (farther from nucleus).
  • Maximum number of electrons in a shell = 2n².

2. Azimuthal / Angular Momentum Quantum Number (l)

  • Denotes the subshell/shape of the orbital within a given shell.
  • Values: l = 0 to (n−1). For each n, there are n possible values of l.
  • l = 0 → s subshell (spherical shape); l = 1 → p (dumbbell); l = 2 → d (cloverleaf); l = 3 → f (complex shape).
  • Also determines the orbital angular momentum: √[l(l+1)] · h/2π

3. Magnetic Quantum Number (m or mₗ)

  • Describes the orientation of the orbital in 3D space relative to a magnetic field.
  • Values: m = −l to +l, including 0 (total of 2l+1 values for each l).
  • For s (l=0): only m=0 → 1 orbital. For p (l=1): m = −1,0,+1 → 3 orbitals (pₓ, p_y, p_z). For d (l=2): 5 values → 5 orbitals. For f (l=3): 7 values → 7 orbitals.

4. Spin Quantum Number (s or mₛ)

  • Describes the direction of spin of an electron about its own axis.
  • Values: +½ (spin up, ↑) or −½ (spin down, ↓) — only two possible values.
  • Proposed by Goudsmit and Uhlenbeck; explains fine splitting of spectral lines and the Pauli Exclusion Principle (Ch.9).
  • Each orbital can hold maximum 2 electrons, which must have opposite spins.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • Memory order: n → shell/size/energy, l → subshell/shape, m → orientation, s → spin.
  • For n=3: l can be 0,1,2 (s,p,d) → 9 orbitals total → max 18 electrons (2n²=18).
  • Common numerical trap: "How many orbitals possible for n=4?" → n² = 16 orbitals → 32 electrons max.
  • Number of orbitals in a subshell = 2l+1 (s=1, p=3, d=5, f=7) — heavily tested.
  • Trap: m values depend on l, NOT on n directly (a frequent confusion in MCQs).

Chapter 9: Electronic Configuration Rules (Aufbau, Pauli, Hund's)

1. Aufbau Principle ("Building-Up" Principle)

  • Electrons are filled into orbitals in order of increasing energy — lowest energy orbitals fill first.
  • Order of filling (by (n+l) rule): 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s, 5f, 6d...
  • (n+l) rule: Orbitals with lower (n+l) value fill first. If (n+l) is equal, the orbital with lower n fills first. E.g., 4s (n+l=4) fills before 3d (n+l=5); for 3p and 4s, both have n+l=4, but 3p (lower n) fills first.

2. Pauli Exclusion Principle

  • No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of all four quantum numbers.
  • Practical consequence: a single orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, and they must have opposite spins (+½ and −½).
  • This is why the same orbital (same n, l, m) electrons must differ in spin (s) to satisfy the principle.

3. Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity

  • Electrons are distributed among the orbitals of a subshell of equal energy (degenerate orbitals) in such a way as to give the maximum number of unpaired electrons with parallel spins.
  • Pairing of electrons in an orbital occurs only after each orbital of that subshell has received one electron.
  • Example: For carbon (2p²) — both electrons go into separate p orbitals (↑ ↑) rather than pairing in one (↑↓).

Exceptions to Aufbau Principle (High-Yield for AFNS)

  • Chromium (Cr, Z=24): Expected [Ar]3d⁴4s² → Actual = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (half-filled d gives extra stability).
  • Copper (Cu, Z=29): Expected [Ar]3d⁹4s² → Actual = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (fully-filled d gives extra stability).
  • General reason: half-filled and fully-filled subshells have greater symmetry, lower electron-electron repulsion, and exchange energy stabilization — making them extra stable.
  • Similar exceptions occur for Mo, Ag, Au (also d⁵s¹ and d¹⁰s¹ patterns) but Cr and Cu are the most commonly tested at this level.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • 4s fills BEFORE 3d (Aufbau/n+l rule) — but when writing configuration for ions, 4s electrons are removed first (e.g., Fe²⁺ = [Ar]3d⁶, not [Ar]3d⁴4s²). This is a VERY high-yield trap.
  • Cr = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ and Cu = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ — memorize these two exceptions cold; they appear almost every year.
  • Hund's rule example: N (2p³) = ↑ ↑ ↑ (three separate orbitals, all unpaired) — maximum multiplicity.
  • Pauli vs Hund distinction: Pauli = max 2 e⁻/orbital with opposite spins. Hund = how electrons distribute among orbitals of SAME subshell before pairing.

Chapter 10: Modern Periodic Table & Shielding Effect

Modern Periodic Law

  • Stated as: "The physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers" — a direct consequence of Moseley's work (Ch.7).
  • The Modern Periodic Table arranges elements in order of increasing atomic number (Z), in 7 horizontal rows (periods) and 18 vertical columns (groups).

Periods & Groups

  • Periods (1–7): Indicate the value of the principal quantum number (n) of the outermost shell. Period number = number of shells.
  • Groups (1–18): Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties.
  • Elements are classified into 4 blocks: s-block (Groups 1-2), p-block (Groups 13-18), d-block (Groups 3-12, transition metals), f-block (lanthanides & actinides).

Shielding (Screening) Effect

  • Inner-shell electrons repel/shield the outer (valence) electrons from the full attractive pull of the nucleus.
  • This reduces the net positive charge experienced by valence electrons — called the Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff).
  • Formula: Zeff = Z − S, where Z = actual nuclear charge (atomic number) and S = shielding/screening constant (≈ number of inner shielding electrons).
  • Down a group: Shielding increases significantly (more shells added) → Zeff on valence electrons stays roughly similar, but electrons are farther away → atomic size increases.
  • Across a period: Shielding stays roughly constant (electrons added to the same shell), but Z increases → Zeff increases → atomic size decreases.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • Period number = number of electron shells = highest value of n for that element.
  • Zeff = Z − S; greater shielding → lower Zeff → weaker hold on valence electrons → larger atomic size, lower ionization energy.
  • Key link: Shielding effect is the master concept behind Chapters 11, 12, and 13 (ionization energy, electronegativity, atomic radius trends all depend on it).
  • Trap: "Same group, same valence electrons" — but shielding differs, so reactivity/size still changes down the group.

Comparison Table: Trends Across a Period vs Down a Group

Factor Across a Period (L → R) Down a Group (Top → Bottom)
Atomic Number (Z) Increases Increases
Number of Shells Constant Increases
Shielding (S) ~Constant Increases
Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff) Increases ~Constant / slight increase

Chapter 11: Ionization Energy & Electron Affinity Trends

Ionization Energy (IE)

  • Definition: The minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound (outermost) electron from an isolated, gaseous, neutral atom in its ground state.
  • X(g) + Energy → X⁺(g) + e⁻   (always endothermic, IE is always positive)
  • Across a period (L → R): IE generally increases — due to increasing Zeff and decreasing atomic size (electrons held more tightly).
  • Down a group: IE generally decreases — due to increasing atomic size and shielding (outer electrons farther from nucleus, easier to remove).

Exceptions to IE Trend (High-Yield)

  • Be > B (Group 2 > Group 13): Be has a stable, fully-filled 2s² configuration; removing an electron from B's 2p¹ (which is higher in energy but less stable to lose due to penetration effects) is actually easier — so IE(B) < IE(Be).
  • N > O (Group 15 > Group 16): N has a stable, half-filled 2p³ configuration (extra stability from symmetry); O's 2p⁴ has one paired orbital, and removing that paired electron is easier due to electron-electron repulsion — so IE(O) < IE(N).
  • Mg > Al and P > S follow the same pattern as Be>B and N>O respectively (full/half-filled subshell stability).

Successive Ionization Energies

  • IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ ... — each successive electron is removed from an increasingly positive ion, requiring more energy.
  • A very large jump between successive IE values indicates that the next electron is being removed from a new (inner, more stable) shell — used to determine the number of valence electrons.

Electron Affinity (EA)

  • Definition: The energy change when an electron is added to a neutral, gaseous atom to form a negative ion (anion).
  • X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g) + Energy   (usually exothermic / energy released → EA conventionally given a negative sign in many texts)
  • Across a period: EA generally becomes more negative (increases in magnitude) — atoms more "eager" to gain electrons due to higher Zeff.
  • Down a group: EA generally becomes less negative (decreases in magnitude) — larger atomic size means the added electron is farther from the nucleus.
  • Exception: Cl has a more negative (higher magnitude) EA than F — despite F being smaller and having higher Zeff. Reason: F's very small atomic size causes significant electron-electron repulsion in its compact 2p orbitals when a new electron is added, making the process less favorable than for the larger Cl atom.
  • Noble gases have EA ≈ 0 or slightly positive (endothermic) — stable full octet means added electron must go into a new, higher-energy shell.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • Memorize all 4 exceptions: Be>B, Mg>Al (Group 2>13) and N>O, P>S (Group 15>16) — all due to full/half-filled stability.
  • EA exception: Cl > F in magnitude (most commonly tested EA exception in the entire syllabus).
  • General trend memory aid: IE & |EA| both increase across a period (left→right) and decrease down a group (top→bottom) — exceptions aside.
  • Trap: IE is ALWAYS positive (endothermic); EA is usually negative (exothermic) but can be positive for noble gases — don't mix up the signs.

Chapter 12: Electronegativity & Metallic Character

Electronegativity (EN)

  • Definition: The relative tendency of an atom in a molecule (a bonded atom) to attract the shared pair of electrons toward itself.
  • Most common scale: Pauling scale — Fluorine (F) has the highest EN value = 4.0 (the highest of all elements).
  • Across a period: EN increases (left → right) — due to increasing Zeff and decreasing atomic size.
  • Down a group: EN decreases — due to increasing atomic size and shielding.
  • Most electronegative element overall = Fluorine (F). Least electronegative (most electropositive) = Cesium/Francium (Cs/Fr).

Metallic Character

  • Definition: The tendency of an atom to lose electrons and form positive ions (cations) — directly related to low ionization energy and low electronegativity.
  • Across a period (L → R): Metallic character decreases (elements become more non-metallic) — as IE and EN increase, the tendency to lose electrons decreases.
  • Down a group: Metallic character increases — as IE decreases, electrons are more easily lost.
  • Result: the most metallic elements are found in the bottom-left of the periodic table (e.g., Cesium, Francium); the most non-metallic elements are in the top-right (e.g., Fluorine, excluding noble gases).

Relationship Summary

  • High IE + High EN + Small size = Non-metallic character (top-right of periodic table).
  • Low IE + Low EN + Large size = Metallic character (bottom-left of periodic table).
  • Metallic character is essentially the inverse trend of electronegativity and ionization energy.

⚡ Quick Revision

  • Fluorine = most electronegative (4.0 on Pauling scale) — one of the most repeated single facts in chemistry MCQs.
  • Metallic character is OPPOSITE to electronegativity trend: increases down a group & decreases across a period (mirror image of EN trend).
  • Diagonal relationship memory aid: Metallic character is highest at bottom-left, non-metallic highest at top-right (excluding noble gases) — forms a diagonal "staircase" of metalloids separating metals and non-metals.
  • Trap: Noble gases technically have very low/undefined EN values on most scales despite being on the far right — often excluded from "most electronegative" comparisons.

Chapter 13: Atomic Radius & Ionic Radius Trends

Atomic Radius

  • Definition: Half the distance between the nuclei of two bonded/adjacent identical atoms (covalent radius) or half the distance between two atoms in contact in a metallic lattice (metallic radius).
  • Across a period (L → R): Atomic radius decreases — number of shells stays the same, but Zeff increases, pulling electrons closer to the nucleus.
  • Down a group: Atomic radius increases — additional electron shells are added, and increased shielding outweighs the increase in nuclear charge.

Ionic Radius

  • Cations (positive ions): Always smaller than their parent atom. Removing electron(s) reduces electron-electron repulsion and often removes an entire shell, and the remaining electrons are pulled in closer by the (now relatively larger) Zeff per electron.
  • Anions (negative ions): Always larger than their parent atom. Adding electron(s) increases electron-electron repulsion, and Zeff per electron decreases, allowing the electron cloud to expand.
  • Order example: Na⁺ < Na (cation smaller than atom); Cl⁻ > Cl (anion larger than atom).

Isoelectronic Species

  • Definition: Species (atoms/ions) having the same number of electrons (same electronic configuration).
  • Among isoelectronic species, radius decreases as nuclear charge (Z) increases — more protons pull the same number of electrons in more tightly.
  • Example series (all 10 electrons): O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺ — radius decreases left to right as Z increases (8→13) while electron count stays constant at 10.

  • * = exceptions exist (see Chapter 11 for IE/EA exceptions: Be>B, N>O, Cl>F EA).
  • Cation always smaller, anion always larger than the respective parent atom — one of the most basic but most-tested facts.
  • Isoelectronic trend: same electron count → more protons = smaller radius. Practice ordering series like N³⁻, O²⁻, F⁻, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺.
