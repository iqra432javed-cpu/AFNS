Chapter 1: Discovery of Electron, Proton and Neutron

Core Concepts

  • Cathode Ray Tube (CRT): A discharge tube containing gas at very low pressure (about 0.01 mm Hg) connected to a high voltage source (10,000 volts), used to study conduction of electricity through gases.
  • J.J. Thomson (1897): Discovered the electron by studying the deflection of cathode rays in electric and magnetic fields, and determined the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of the electron.
  • Cathode Rays: Streams of electrons emitted from the cathode of a discharge tube; they travel in straight lines and produce fluorescence on the glass wall opposite the cathode.
  • Properties of Electron: Charge = -1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs; Mass = 9.1 × 10⁻²⁸ g (about 1/1837 of the mass of a hydrogen atom).
  • Robert Millikan (1909): Determined the actual charge on an electron through the famous Oil Drop Experiment.
  • Positive Rays (Canal Rays): Discovered by E. Goldstein (1886) using a perforated cathode; these rays travel opposite to cathode rays and consist of positively charged particles.
  • Discovery of Proton: The lightest positive particle obtained when hydrogen gas is used in the discharge tube was named proton, with charge +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C and mass approximately 1 atomic mass unit.
  • Discovery of Neutron: Predicted by Rutherford and discovered experimentally by James Chadwick (1932); a neutral particle with mass nearly equal to that of a proton.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Cathode rays travel from cathode to anode and are streams of electrons.
  • e/m ratio of electron = 1.7588 × 10⁸ C/g (Thomson's experiment).
  • Millikan's oil drop experiment gave the exact charge of electron.
  • Canal rays depend on the gas used in the discharge tube.
  • Neutron has no charge; mass nearly equal to proton.

Chapter 2: Cathode Rays & Canal Rays Properties

Core Concepts

  • Nature of Cathode Rays: Stream of fast-moving electrons emitted from the cathode; they travel in straight lines perpendicular to the cathode surface.
  • Characteristics of Cathode Rays: Travel in straight lines, produce shadows of objects placed in their path, cause fluorescence in glass and certain materials (e.g., zinc sulphide), possess kinetic energy, can rotate a small paddle wheel, are deflected by electric and magnetic fields toward the positive plate, and can ionize gases.
  • Nature of Canal Rays (Positive Rays): Stream of positively charged particles moving from anode towards cathode, formed when electrons from cathode rays knock out electrons from gas atoms in the tube.
  • Characteristics of Canal Rays: Travel in straight lines, deflected by electric and magnetic fields toward the negative plate, e/m value depends on the nature of the gas used (unlike cathode rays which are always the same regardless of gas), and consist of particles heavier than electrons.
  • Effect of Electric Field: Cathode rays bend toward the positive plate (confirming negative charge); canal rays bend toward the negative plate (confirming positive charge).
  • Effect of Magnetic Field: Both types of rays are deflected according to the direction of the applied magnetic field, following the rules of charged particle motion (Fleming's rules).
  • Applications: Cathode ray principle is used in television picture tubes, oscilloscopes, and X-ray tubes; canal ray studies led to the discovery of isotopes using mass spectrometry.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Cathode rays are independent of the gas and electrode material — always electrons.
  • Canal rays' e/m ratio is maximum when hydrogen gas is used.
  • Both rays travel in straight lines and cast shadows.
  • Cathode ray tube principle → basis of television and CRT monitors.

Chapter 3: Discovery of Neutron (Chadwick's Experiment)

Core Concepts

  • Background: Rutherford proposed in 1920 that besides protons, the nucleus must contain another neutral particle of mass nearly equal to a proton to account for atomic mass.
  • Bothe and Becker's Observation (1930): When beryllium was bombarded with alpha particles, a highly penetrating radiation was emitted, initially thought to be gamma rays.
  • Chadwick's Experimental Setup (1932): A polonium source emitted alpha particles which struck a beryllium target; the radiation produced was directed onto a paraffin wax block (rich in hydrogen).
  • Nuclear Reaction: ⁹Be + ⁴He (alpha) → ¹²C + ¹n (neutron)
  • Observation: The radiation knocked out protons from the paraffin wax with high velocity, which could be detected and measured.
  • Conclusion: The radiation consisted of neutral particles with mass approximately equal to that of a proton — Chadwick named this particle the neutron.
  • Properties of Neutron: Charge = 0 (neutral); Mass = 1.675 × 10⁻²⁴ g (approximately 1 amu); found in the nucleus along with protons.
  • Importance: Explained the existence of isotopes (atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons); completed the basic model of atomic structure consisting of protons, neutrons, and electrons.
Step Description
Source Polonium (alpha emitter)
Target Beryllium metal
Detector Material Paraffin wax (hydrogen-rich)
Result High-speed protons ejected from wax
Conclusion Neutral particle (neutron) of mass ≈ 1 amu discovered

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Neutron discovered by James Chadwick in 1932.
  • Neutron produced by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles.
  • Neutron mass ≈ 1.675 × 10⁻²⁴ g, charge = zero.
  • Discovery of neutron explained isotopes.

Chapter 4: Rutherford's Atomic Model & Its Defects

Core Concepts

  • Gold Foil (Alpha Particle Scattering) Experiment (1911): Rutherford bombarded a thin gold foil (about 0.0004 cm thick) with high-speed alpha particles emitted from a radioactive source, using a circular zinc sulphide (ZnS) fluorescent screen to detect scattered particles.
  • Observations: Most alpha particles passed straight through the foil without deflection; a few were deflected at small angles; a very small number (about 1 in 20,000) bounced back at angles greater than 90°.
  • Conclusions Drawn: Since most particles passed undeflected, the atom is mostly empty space; since a few were deflected sharply, there exists a small, dense, positively charged center called the nucleus; since the nucleus repelled positively charged alpha particles, it must carry a positive charge and contain almost all the mass of the atom.
  • Rutherford's Nuclear Model (Postulates): The atom consists of a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center containing protons (and later neutrons); electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular orbits at high speed, similar to planets orbiting the sun; the nucleus occupies a very small volume compared to the total volume of the atom; the atom is electrically neutral overall.
  • Defects of Rutherford's Model: According to classical electromagnetic theory, an accelerating (revolving) charged particle must continuously emit energy/radiation; thus the electron should lose energy continuously and spiral into the nucleus, making the atom unstable — but atoms are known to be stable. It also failed to explain the line spectrum of hydrogen (why atoms emit light only at specific wavelengths). It gave no information about the arrangement and energies of electrons in the atom.
Observation Conclusion
Most α-particles pass straight through Atom is mostly empty space
Few particles deflected at small angles Presence of a concentrated positive charge
Very few bounced back (>90°) Existence of small, dense, heavy nucleus

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Gold foil experiment (1911) led to discovery of the nucleus.
  • Nucleus is extremely small but contains almost the entire mass of the atom.
  • Major defect: could not explain stability of atom (electron should spiral into nucleus per classical physics).
  • Could not explain the hydrogen line spectrum.
  • These defects paved the way for Bohr's atomic theory.

Chapter 5: Bohr's Atomic Theory

Core Concepts

  • Background: Proposed by Niels Bohr (1913) to overcome the defects of Rutherford's model, based on Planck's Quantum Theory and the hydrogen line spectrum.
  • Postulate 1 (Stationary Orbits): An electron revolves around the nucleus only in certain fixed circular paths called orbits or shells, each having a definite energy. As long as the electron remains in an orbit, it does not radiate energy.
  • Postulate 2 (Quantization of Angular Momentum): The angular momentum of an electron is quantized and is an integral multiple of h/2π: mvr = nh/2π, where n = 1, 2, 3... (principal quantum number).
  • Postulate 3 (Energy Levels / Shells): Each orbit corresponds to a definite energy level; orbits are numbered n = 1, 2, 3... (K, L, M, N shells) starting from the nucleus, with energy increasing as n increases.
  • Postulate 4 (Emission/Absorption of Energy): An electron can jump from a lower energy orbit to a higher one by absorbing energy, and from a higher to a lower orbit by emitting energy as a photon, where the energy difference ΔE = E₂ - E₁ = hν.
  • Hydrogen Spectrum: When an electric discharge is passed through hydrogen gas, excited electrons jump to higher orbits and then fall back, emitting radiation of specific wavelengths that appear as line spectra (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen, Brackett, and Pfund series).
  • Balmer Series: Lies in the visible region of the spectrum, produced when electrons fall from higher energy levels (n > 2) to n = 2.
  • Limitations: Bohr's theory could explain only the spectrum of hydrogen (single-electron system) and could not satisfactorily explain the spectra of multi-electron atoms or the splitting of spectral lines in magnetic/electric fields.

  • Bohr's model introduced quantized circular orbits with fixed energy.
  • mvr = nh/2π is the key quantization equation.
  • Balmer series is the only series visible to the naked eye.
  • Bohr's theory works only for hydrogen-like (single electron) species.

Chapter 6: Planck's Quantum Theory

Core Concepts

  • Background: Classical (wave) theory of radiation failed to explain the distribution of energy in the spectrum of a black body, known as the "ultraviolet catastrophe."
  • Max Planck (1900): Proposed that energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously but in small discrete packets called quanta (singular: quantum).
  • Quantum: The smallest discrete amount of energy that can be emitted or absorbed in the form of electromagnetic radiation.
  • Planck's Equation: E = hν, where E = energy of one quantum, h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s), and ν = frequency of radiation.
  • Relation with Wavelength: Since ν = c/λ, the equation can also be written as E = hc/λ, where c = speed of light and λ = wavelength.
  • Total Energy: The total energy emitted or absorbed is always a whole-number multiple of hν, i.e., E = nhν, where n is an integer (1, 2, 3...).
  • Importance: Provided the theoretical foundation for Bohr's atomic model, explained the photoelectric effect (Einstein, 1905), and marked the beginning of quantum mechanics.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Planck's Quantum Theory: energy is emitted/absorbed in discrete packets (quanta).
  • E = hν is the most important formula — remember h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s.
  • Energy is directly proportional to frequency and inversely proportional to wavelength.
  • This theory laid the foundation of Bohr's atomic model.

Chapter 7: X-Rays & Moseley's Law

Core Concepts

  • Discovery of X-Rays: Discovered by Wilhelm Röntgen (1895) while experimenting with cathode ray tubes; he observed a highly penetrating, invisible radiation produced when cathode rays struck a metal target (anode).
  • Production of X-Rays: Produced when high-speed electrons (cathode rays) strike a heavy metal target (such as tungsten or molybdenum) inside an evacuated tube, causing sudden deceleration and emission of electromagnetic radiation of very short wavelength.
  • Characteristics of X-Rays: Electromagnetic radiation with very short wavelength (0.01 to 10 nm); travel in straight lines with the speed of light; not deflected by electric or magnetic fields (since they have no charge); highly penetrating; ionize gases; affect photographic plates; cause fluorescence in certain materials.
  • Atomic Number Concept: The atomic number (Z) of an element represents the number of protons in the nucleus (and equals the number of electrons in a neutral atom); it determines the chemical identity of an element.
  • Moseley's Experiment (1913): Henry Moseley studied the X-ray spectra of various elements by bombarding different metal targets with high-energy electrons and measuring the frequency of the emitted characteristic X-rays.
  • Moseley's Law: The square root of the frequency (ν) of characteristic X-rays emitted by an element is directly proportional to its atomic number (Z): √ν = a(Z - b), where a and b are constants.
  • Significance: Moseley's work established that atomic number, not atomic mass, is the fundamental property that determines the position of an element in the periodic table; it also led to the correction of the periodic table arrangement and the prediction of undiscovered elements.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • X-rays discovered by Röntgen in 1895 using a cathode ray discharge tube.
  • X-rays are electromagnetic waves, hence not deflected by magnetic/electric fields.
  • Moseley's Law: √ν ∝ (Z - b), where Z = atomic number.
  • Moseley proved atomic number (not atomic mass) determines periodic table arrangement.

Chapter 8: Quantum Numbers

Core Concepts

  • Definition: Quantum numbers are a set of four numbers that describe the position, energy, shape of orbital, and orientation of an electron in an atom.
  • Principal Quantum Number (n): Denotes the main energy level (shell) in which an electron is located; n = 1, 2, 3, 4... (corresponding to K, L, M, N shells); determines the size and energy of the orbital — larger n means higher energy and larger size; maximum number of electrons in a shell = 2n².
  • Azimuthal (Angular Momentum / Subsidiary) Quantum Number (l): Denotes the subshell and the shape of the orbital; values range from 0 to (n-1); l = 0 (s, spherical), l = 1 (p, dumbbell), l = 2 (d, complex/cloverleaf), l = 3 (f); determines the shape of the electron cloud.
  • Magnetic Quantum Number (m or mₗ): Denotes the orientation of the orbital in space relative to the three axes (x, y, z); values range from -l to +l, including zero, giving (2l+1) possible orientations for each subshell.
  • Spin Quantum Number (s or mₛ): Denotes the direction of spin of the electron on its own axis; has only two possible values: +1/2 (clockwise/spin up) and -1/2 (anticlockwise/spin down).
  • Electron Capacity of Shells and Subshells: Each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons (with opposite spins); s subshell holds max 2 electrons, p subshell holds max 6, d subshell holds max 10, f subshell holds max 14.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Maximum electrons in a shell = 2n² (e.g., n=1 → 2, n=2 → 8, n=3 → 18).
  • l = 0,1,2,3 correspond to s, p, d, f subshells respectively.
  • Number of orbitals in a subshell = (2l+1).
  • Spin quantum number has only two values: +1/2 and -1/2.

Chapter 9: Electronic Configuration Rules

Core Concepts

  • Electronic Configuration: The arrangement of electrons in different shells, subshells, and orbitals of an atom.
  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons are filled into orbitals in order of increasing energy, i.e., the orbital with the lowest energy is filled first before electrons enter orbitals of higher energy.
  • Order of Filling (Aufbau order): 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s, 5f, 6d...
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of all four quantum numbers; consequently, an orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons, and these two electrons must have opposite spins.
  • Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity: Electrons are distributed among the orbitals of a subshell in such a way as to give the maximum number of unpaired electrons with parallel spins; pairing of electrons in an orbital of a subshell does not occur until each orbital of that subshell has received one electron.
  • Examples: Carbon (Z=6): 1s² 2s² 2p² (the two 2p electrons occupy separate orbitals with parallel spins as per Hund's Rule). Sodium (Z=11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹. Chromium (Z=24): exhibits an exception — 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁵ 4s¹ (extra stability of half-filled d-subshell).
RuleStatement (Short)
Aufbau PrincipleFill lowest energy orbitals first
Pauli Exclusion PrincipleMax 2 electrons per orbital, opposite spins
Hund's RuleOne electron per orbital before pairing; parallel spins

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Order of filling follows (n + l) rule — lower (n+l) value fills first.
  • 4s is filled before 3d despite 3d having lower n (due to lower n+l value).
  • Cr and Cu are common exceptions due to extra stability of half-filled/fully-filled d-orbitals.
  • Hund's Rule maximizes unpaired electrons → maximum stability.

Chapter 10: Modern Periodic Table & Shielding Effect

Core Concepts

  • Modern Periodic Law: The physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers (Moseley/Modern Periodic Table, based on Mendeleev's earlier work).
  • Periods: Horizontal rows in the periodic table (7 periods); indicate the number of shells (energy levels) occupied by electrons; elements in the same period have the same number of principal shells.
  • Groups: Vertical columns in the periodic table (18 groups); elements in the same group have similar valence shell electronic configurations and therefore similar chemical properties.
  • Blocks: The periodic table is divided into four blocks based on the subshell being filled — s-block (Groups 1-2, alkali and alkaline earth metals), p-block (Groups 13-18), d-block (Groups 3-12, transition elements), and f-block (lanthanides and actinides, inner transition elements).
  • Shielding (Screening) Effect: The phenomenon in which inner-shell electrons shield/screen the outer (valence) electrons from the full positive charge of the nucleus, reducing the net attractive force experienced by the valence electrons.
  • Effective Nuclear Charge (Z_eff): The actual nuclear charge experienced by an electron, after accounting for shielding by other electrons; Z_eff = Z (actual nuclear charge) - σ (shielding constant).
  • Trend of Shielding Effect: Shielding effect increases down a group (more inner shells); shielding effect remains roughly constant across a period (same number of inner shells), but effective nuclear charge increases across a period due to increasing nuclear charge.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Period number = number of shells; Group number relates to valence electrons.
  • Modern periodic table arranged by increasing atomic number (Moseley's contribution).
  • Shielding effect increases down a group, reducing nuclear attraction on valence electrons.
  • Z_eff = Z - σ (effective nuclear charge formula).

Chapter 11: Ionization Energy & Electron Affinity Trends

Core Concepts

  • Ionization Energy (Ionization Potential): The minimum amount of energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated, neutral gaseous atom in its ground state, converting it into a positive ion (cation).
  • Successive Ionization Energies: The first ionization energy (IE₁) is always less than the second (IE₂), which is less than the third (IE₃), because removing an electron from an already positively charged ion requires more energy.
  • Factors Affecting Ionization Energy: Atomic size (larger size → lower ionization energy, since outer electrons are farther from the nucleus); nuclear charge (greater nuclear charge → higher ionization energy); shielding effect (more shielding → lower ionization energy); and electronic configuration (extra stability of fully-filled or half-filled subshells increases ionization energy).
  • Periodic Trend of Ionization Energy: Generally increases across a period (left to right) due to increasing nuclear charge and decreasing atomic size; generally decreases down a group due to increasing atomic size and shielding effect.
  • Electron Affinity: The amount of energy released when an electron is added to an isolated, neutral gaseous atom in its ground state to form a negative ion (anion).
  • Factors Affecting Electron Affinity: Atomic size (smaller size → higher electron affinity, as added electron is closer to nucleus); nuclear charge (greater nuclear charge → higher electron affinity); and electronic configuration (atoms with stable configurations, like noble gases, have very low/negative electron affinity).
  • Periodic Trend of Electron Affinity: Generally increases across a period (left to right), with halogens having the highest electron affinity; generally decreases down a group.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Noble gases have the highest ionization energies in their periods (stable configuration).
  • IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃ for any element (successive ionization energies always increase).
  • Halogens have the highest electron affinities (one electron short of stable octet).
  • Noble gases have nearly zero/negative electron affinity.

Chapter 12: Electronegativity & Metallic Character

Core Concepts

  • Electronegativity: The tendency or ability of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared pair of electrons (bonding electrons) towards itself.
  • Pauling Scale: The most common scale for measuring electronegativity, proposed by Linus Pauling, ranging approximately from 0.7 (Cesium/Francium, lowest) to 4.0 (Fluorine, highest).
  • Trend Across a Period: Electronegativity generally increases from left to right across a period due to increasing nuclear charge and decreasing atomic size, which increases the attraction for bonding electrons.
  • Trend Down a Group: Electronegativity generally decreases down a group due to increasing atomic size and shielding effect, which reduces the nuclear attraction for bonding electrons.
  • Metallic Character: The tendency of an atom to lose electrons and form positive ions (cations); metals are electropositive in nature.
  • Trend of Metallic Character Across a Period: Decreases from left to right (metals → metalloids → non-metals) as ionization energy increases, making it harder to lose electrons.
  • Trend of Metallic Character Down a Group: Increases down a group as atomic size increases and ionization energy decreases, making it easier to lose electrons.
  • Comparison: Electronegativity and metallic character are inversely related — elements with high electronegativity (non-metals) have low metallic character, while elements with low electronegativity (metals) have high metallic character.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Fluorine is the most electronegative element (value = 4.0 on Pauling scale).
  • Cesium and Francium are the most electropositive (least electronegative) elements.
  • Metallic character is highest at the bottom-left of the periodic table (e.g., Cesium).
  • Non-metallic character is highest at the top-right (e.g., Fluorine, excluding noble gases).

Chapter 13: Atomic Radius & Ionic Radius Trends

Core Concepts

  • Atomic Radius: The distance from the center of the nucleus to the outermost shell containing electrons (valence shell); cannot be measured directly due to the absence of a sharp boundary, so it is determined indirectly.
  • Covalent Radius: One-half of the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms joined by a single covalent bond; used for non-metals.
  • Metallic Radius: One-half of the distance between the nuclei of two adjacent atoms in a metallic crystal lattice; used for metals.
  • Ionic Radius: The effective distance from the nucleus of an ion to the point up to which it influences its electron cloud; measured for cations and anions.
  • Cations vs. Parent Atom: A cation (positive ion) is always smaller than its parent neutral atom, because the loss of electron(s) reduces electron-electron repulsion and increases the effective nuclear charge per electron, pulling the remaining electrons closer.
  • Anions vs. Parent Atom: An anion (negative ion) is always larger than its parent neutral atom, because the addition of electron(s) increases electron-electron repulsion and decreases the effective nuclear charge per electron, causing the electron cloud to expand.
  • Trend of Atomic Radius Across a Period: Generally decreases from left to right due to increasing nuclear charge (which pulls electrons closer) while the number of shells remains the same.
  • Trend of Atomic Radius Down a Group: Increases down a group due to the addition of new shells (increase in n), which outweighs the increase in nuclear charge.
  • Isoelectronic Species: Ions or atoms having the same number of electrons; among isoelectronic species, the one with the higher nuclear charge (atomic number) has the smaller radius.

AFNS Quick Revision Points

  • Atomic radius decreases across a period and increases down a group.
  • Cation < Parent Atom < Anion (general size relationship).
  • Covalent radius = half the bond length between two identical bonded atoms.
  • For isoelectronic species, higher atomic number → smaller radius.
