Chapter 1 — Discovery of Electron, Proton & Neutron

The discovery of subatomic particles changed the concept of Dalton's indivisible atom. Scientists proved that atoms contain smaller particles called electrons, protons and neutrons.

Discovery of Electron — J.J Thomson

In 1897, J.J Thomson performed the cathode ray discharge tube experiment and discovered electrons.

Cathode Ray Tube Experiment

  • A glass tube containing gas at low pressure was used.
  • High voltage was applied between two electrodes.
  • Rays produced from cathode travelled towards anode.

Observations

  • Cathode rays travel in straight line.
  • They produce fluorescence on glass surface.
  • They are deflected towards positive plate.
  • They possess kinetic energy.

Chapter 3 — Discovery of Neutron (Chadwick Experiment)

James Chadwick discovered neutron in 1932 by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles.

Experiment

  • Alpha particles were fired on beryllium sheet.
  • A new highly penetrating radiation was produced.
  • Radiation had no electric charge.

Conclusion

The radiation consisted of neutral particles called neutrons.

Property Neutron
Charge 0
Mass 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
Location Nucleus
AFNS Important:
Neutron discovery solved the problem of atomic mass.

Chapter 4 — Rutherford's Atomic Model & Defects

Ernest Rutherford performed the famous gold foil experiment in 1911 to understand the internal structure of atom.

Gold Foil Experiment

  • Alpha particles were directed towards a thin gold foil.
  • A fluorescent screen detected the scattered particles.
  • Most particles passed straight through the foil.
  • Few particles were deflected.
  • Very few bounced back.

Observations & Conclusions

Observation Conclusion
Most particles passed through Atom is mostly empty space
Few particles deflected Positive charge concentrated in small region
Few returned back Nucleus is dense

Rutherford Model

  • Atom contains a small positively charged nucleus.
  • Electrons revolve around nucleus.
  • Most of atom is empty space.

Defects of Rutherford Model

  • Could not explain stability of atom.
  • According to classical physics electrons should lose energy and fall into nucleus.
  • Could not explain line spectrum of elements.
AFNS Favourite:
Gold foil experiment → Rutherford → Nuclear model

Chapter 5 — Bohr's Atomic Theory & Postulates

Niels Bohr improved Rutherford's model by introducing fixed energy levels for electrons.

Postulates

  • Electrons revolve around nucleus in fixed circular paths called orbits.
  • Each orbit has a definite energy.
  • Electrons do not lose energy while staying in an allowed orbit.
  • Energy is absorbed or emitted when electrons jump between levels.
Shell Name Maximum Electrons
1 K 2
2 L 8
3 M 18

Limitations

  • Works mainly for hydrogen atom.
  • Cannot explain spectra of multi-electron atoms.
AFNS:
Bohr explained hydrogen spectrum.

Chapter 6 — Planck's Quantum Theory

Max Planck proposed quantum theory in 1900 to explain black body radiation.

Main Points

  • Energy is emitted or absorbed in small packets.
  • These packets are called quanta.
  • Energy depends on frequency of radiation.
Planck Equation:

E = hν

h = Planck constant
ν = frequency

Important Relation

Frequency Energy
Higher Higher energy
Lower Lower energy

Chapter 7 — X-Rays & Moseley's Law

X-Rays

X-rays are high energy electromagnetic radiations discovered during experiments with cathode rays.

Properties

  • Travel in straight lines.
  • Have high penetrating power.
  • Can affect photographic plates.
  • Are not deflected by electric field.

Moseley's Law

Henry Moseley proved that atomic number is the fundamental property of elements.

Old Arrangement Modern Arrangement
Atomic mass Atomic number
AFNS Point:
Modern periodic table is arranged according to atomic number.

Chapter 9 — Electronic Configuration Rules

Electronic configuration is the distribution of electrons in different orbitals.

Aufbau Principle

Electrons occupy orbitals of lower energy before higher energy.

Order Orbital
1 1s
2 2s
3 2p
4 3s

Pauli Exclusion Principle

No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers.

Hund's Rule

Electrons occupy empty orbitals singly before pairing.

Example:
Nitrogen:
1s² 2s² 2p³
Three unpaired electrons.
