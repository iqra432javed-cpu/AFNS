Chapter 1: Discovery of Electron, Proton and Neutron

  • Discovery of Electron: Identified through discharge tube experiments by J.J. Thomson (1897). He measured the specific charge ratio ($e/m$) of the particle.
  • Naming of Electron: The term "electron" was introduced by G. Johnstone Stoney.
  • Discovery of Proton: Observed by E. Goldstein (1886) using a perforated cathode in a discharge tube, which revealed positive rays (canal rays).
  • Discovery of Neutron: Discovered by James Chadwick (1932) by bombarding light elements with alpha particles.
Particle Absolute Charge (C) Relative Charge Mass (kg) Mass (amu)
Electron ($e^-$) $-1.6022 \times 10^{-19}$ $-1$ $9.1093 \times 10^{-31}$ $0.000548$
Proton ($p^+$) $+1.6022 \times 10^{-19}$ $+1$ $1.6726 \times 10^{-27}$ $1.007276$
Neutron ($n^0$) $0$ $0$ $1.6749 \times 10^{-27}$ $1.008665$
AFNS High-Yield Tip: The $e/m$ ratio of an electron is a constant value equal to $1.7588 \times 10^{11} \text{ C/kg}$. Protons are approximately 1836 times heavier than electrons, while neutrons are the heaviest subatomic particles among the three.

Chapter 2: Cathode Rays & Canal Rays Properties

  • Cathode Rays Origins: Emitted perpendicular to the cathode surface when high voltage ($\approx 5\text{--}10\text{ kV}$) is applied under low pressure ($\approx 0.01\text{ torr}$).
  • Mechanical Effects: Turn a small pinwheel placed in their path, confirming they possess momentum and are material particles.
  • Canal Rays Generation: Produced when high-speed cathode rays collide with residual gas molecules in the tube, knocking out electrons and leaving behind positive gaseous ions.
  • Charge-to-Mass Variation: The $e/m$ value of cathode rays remains constant regardless of the gas inside. Conversely, the $e/m$ ratio of canal rays depends entirely on the identity of the gas used.
Property Cathode Rays Canal Rays (Positive Rays)
Composition Stream of high-energy electrons Gaseous cations (positive ions)
Deflection Deflects toward the positive plate Deflects toward the negative plate
Maximum $e/m$ Constant for all gases Highest when Hydrogen ($H_2$) gas is used
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Canal rays do not originate directly from the anode. They are formed in the space between the electrodes due to gas ionization. Hydrogen gas yields the lightest positive ray particle (the proton).

Chapter 3: Discovery of Neutron (Chadwick's Experiment)

  • The Experiment: In 1932, James Chadwick bombarded a thin target of Beryllium ($Be$) with high-velocity alpha particles ($\alpha$) emitted from a Polonium source.
  • Observation: Highly penetrating, neutral radiations were emitted. These radiations could knock out high-energy protons from paraffin wax.
  • Nuclear Reaction: The process is represented by the nuclear equation: $$^{4}_{2}\text{He} + ^{9}_{4}\text{Be} \rightarrow ^{12}_{6}\text{C} + ^{1}_{0}\text{n}$$
  • Stability: A free neutron is unstable outside the nucleus and spontaneously decays into a proton, an electron, and an antineutrino with a half-life of roughly 10.3 minutes.
Property Free Neutron Decay Products Penetrating Power Compared to $\alpha, \beta$
Value / Detail Proton ($p^+$), Electron ($e^-$), and Antineutrino ($\bar{\nu}_e$) Extremely high due to lack of electrostatic charge
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Neutrons cannot cause direct ionization in gases because they carry no net charge. Instead, they induce ionization indirectly via collisions with light nuclei.

Chapter 4: Rutherford's Atomic Model & Defects

  • Alpha Scattering Experiment: Bombarded a $4 \times 10^{-5}\text{ cm}$ thick gold foil with alpha particles. Most passed straight through, but 1 in 20,000 deflected by angles greater than $90^\circ$.
  • Core Deductions: The majority of atomic volume is empty space. The atom's entire positive charge and mass are concentrated in a dense core called the nucleus.
  • Stability Defect: Based on classical electrodynamics, an accelerating charged electron must continuously radiate energy, causing its orbit to shrink until it collapses into the nucleus.
  • Spectral Defect: The continuous loss of energy should yield a continuous spectrum, but atoms produce a sharp line spectrum.
Experimental Observation Rutherford's Structural Conclusion
Most $\alpha$ particles pass straight through Most of the atomic space is completely empty
A few $\alpha$ particles undergo large deflections A dense, positive nucleus occupies the center
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Rutherford's planetary model failed because it relied on classical physics instead of quantum principles, leaving it unable to explain atomic stability or discrete line spectra.

Chapter 5: Bohr's Atomic Theory & Postulates

  • Quantized Orbits: Electrons revolve around the nucleus only in specific non-radiating paths called stationary states or orbits.
  • Angular Momentum Quantization: The angular momentum ($mvr$) of an electron is an integral multiple of $\frac{h}{2\pi}$: $$mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \dots)$$
  • Energy Transitions: Energy is emitted or absorbed only when an electron jumps from one quantized orbit to another: $$\Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = h\nu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$
  • Radius Formula (Hydrogen): The radius of the $n$-th orbit is given by: $$r_n = a_0 \cdot n^2 = 0.529 \times n^2 \text{ \AA}$$
Orbit Number ($n$) Radius ($r_n$ in \AA) Energy State ($E_n$ for Hydrogen)
$n = 1$ (Ground State) $0.529\text{ \AA}$ $-13.6 \text{ eV} \quad (-1312.36 \text{ kJ/mol})$
$n = 2$ (1st Excited) $2.116\text{ \AA}$ $-3.4 \text{ eV}$
$n = 3$ (2nd Excited) $4.761\text{ \AA}$ $-1.51 \text{ eV}$
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Bohr's model applies only to monoelectronic systems like $\text{H}$, $\text{He}^+$, and $\text{Li}^{2+}$. It cannot account for the spectra of multi-electron atoms or the splitting of spectral lines in magnetic fields (the Zeeman effect).

Chapter 6: Planck's Quantum Theory

  • Discrete Energy Transfer: Energy is emitted or absorbed by matter in discrete, localized packets called quanta. In the case of light, these packets are called photons.
  • Proportionality Law: The energy ($E$) of a photon is directly proportional to its frequency ($\nu$): $$E = h\nu$$
  • Wave Equations: Since $\nu = \frac{c}{\lambda}$ and wavenumber $\bar{\nu} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$, energy can also be expressed as: $$E = \frac{hc}{\lambda} = hc\bar{\nu}$$
  • Planck's Constant: The proportionality factor $h$ has a value of $6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$ (or $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-1}$).
Variable Connection Mathematical Proportionality Relationship
Energy vs Frequency ($E$ vs $\nu$) Directly Proportional ($E \propto \nu$)
Energy vs Wavelength ($E$ vs $\lambda$) Inversely Proportional ($E \propto \frac{1}{\lambda}$)
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Photons have zero rest mass. As wavelength ($\lambda$) increases, both the frequency and the total energy carried by the photon decrease.

Chapter 7: X-Rays & Atomic Number (Moseley's Law)

  • Production: Discovered by W.C. Roentgen. Generated when high-velocity electrons strike a heavy metal target (anode) inside a vacuum tube.
  • Moseley's Discovery: In 1913, H. Moseley discovered that the square root of the frequency ($\sqrt{\nu}$) of the emitted characteristic X-rays is directly proportional to the atomic number ($Z$) of the target metal.
  • Mathematical Formulation: $$\sqrt{\nu} = a(Z - b)$$ Where $a$ is a proportionality constant and $b$ is the screening constant.
  • Impact: This law shifted the basis of classification in the periodic table from atomic mass to atomic number ($Z$).
X-Ray Spectral Series Electronic Transition Origin Relative Energy / Penetrating Power
$K_{\alpha}$ Series Transition from $L$-shell ($n=2$) to $K$-shell ($n=1$) Higher energy and higher penetrating intensity
$L_{\alpha}$ Series Transition from $M$-shell ($n=3$) to $L$-shell ($n=2$) Lower energy and lower relative penetration
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Moseley's law helped establish the correct positions of rare-earth elements and cleared up anomalies in the periodic table, such as the sequencing of Potassium ($Z=19$) and Argon ($Z=18$).

Chapter 8: Quantum Numbers

  • Principal Quantum Number ($n$): Defines the main energy level or shell. Values: $n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$ (corresponding to shells $K, L, M, N$). Dictates the size of the orbital.
  • Azimuthal Quantum Number ($l$): Defines the shape of the orbital or subshell. Values: $l = 0$ to $(n-1)$.
    • $l=0$ ($s$, spherical), • $l=1$ ($p$, dumbbell), • $l=2$ ($d$, cloverleaf), • $l=3$ ($f$, complex).
  • Magnetic Quantum Number ($m_l$): Dictates spatial orientation of orbitals in a magnetic field. Values: $m_l = -l$ to $+l$ (Total orientations = $2l + 1$).
  • Spin Quantum Number ($m_s$): Describes the intrinsic spin rotation of the electron. Values: $+\frac{1}{2}$ (clockwise) or $-\frac{1}{2}$ (counter-clockwise).
Subshell Designation ($l$) Total Number of Orbitals ($2l+1$) Maximum Electron Capacity ($4l+2$)
$s$ ($l = 0$) 1 orbital 2 electrons
$p$ ($l = 1$) 3 orbitals 6 electrons
$d$ ($l = 2$) 5 orbitals 10 electrons
$f$ ($l = 3$) 7 orbitals 14 electrons
AFNS High-Yield Tip: The total number of orbitals in a shell with principle quantum number $n$ is $n^2$, and the maximum electron capacity is given by the formula $2n^2$.

Chapter 9: Electronic Configuration Rules

  • Aufbau Principle: Orbitals are filled in order of increasing energy, according to the $(n+l)$ rule. Lower $(n+l)$ values are filled first. If values are equal, the orbital with the lower $n$ value is filled first.
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in the same atom can have the exact same set of all four quantum numbers. An orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity: When filling degenerate orbitals (orbitals with the same energy), electrons enter them singly with parallel spins before they begin to pair up.
Element Expected Configuration Actual Stable Configuration Underlying Reason
Chromium ($_{24}\text{Cr}$) $[\text{Ar}] 3d^4 4s^2$ $[\text{Ar}] 3d^5 4s^1$ Symmetrical stability of half-filled subshells
Copper ($_{29}\text{Cu}$) $[\text{Ar}] 3d^9 4s^2$ $[\text{Ar}] 3d^{10} 4s^1$ Extra stability of fully-filled subshells
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Watch for anomalous configurations in Transition Elements. Half-filled ($d^5$) and fully-filled ($d^{10}$) subshells are lower in energy and more stable due to their symmetrical electron distribution and high exchange energy.

Chapter 10: Modern Periodic Table & Shielding Effect

  • Modern Periodic Law: Elements are arranged by increasing atomic number. This structure contains 7 Periods (horizontal rows) and 8 Groups (vertical columns, subdivided into A and B sub-groups).
  • Shielding Effect: Inner-shell electrons act as a screen, shielding the outermost valence electrons from the full attractive pull of the nuclear charge.
  • Period Trend: Stays constant or decreases slightly across a period because the number of inner shielding shells remains the same while the nuclear charge increases.
  • Group Trend: Increases down a group due to the sequential addition of new electron shells.
Block Identification Outer Valence Configuration Periodic Classification Category
s-block $ns^{1\text{--}2}$ Reactive metals (Groups IA, IIA)
p-block $ns^2 np^{1\text{--}6}$ Non-metals, Metalloids, Halogens, Noble gases
d-block $(n-1)d^{1\text{--}10} ns^{1\text{--}2}$ Outer Transition Elements
AFNS High-Yield Tip: An increased shielding effect down a group makes it easier for an atom to lose its valence electrons, which directly increases its chemical reactivity as a metal.

Chapter 11: Ionization Energy & Electron Affinity Trends

  • Ionization Energy (IE): The minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated, gaseous atom in its ground state: $$\text{M}_{(g)} + \text{IE} \rightarrow \text{M}^+_{(g)} + e^-$$
  • Electron Affinity (EA): The energy change (usually exothermic for the first electron) that occurs when an electron is added to an isolated gaseous atom: $$\text{X}_{(g)} + e^- \rightarrow \text{X}^-_{(g)} + \text{Energy (EA)}$$
  • Period Variation: Both IE and EA increase from left to right across a period due to an increased effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$) and a decrease in atomic size.
  • Group Variation: Both IE and EA decrease down a group because atomic radii expand and shielding increases.
Property Direction Across a Period (Left to Right) Down a Group (Top to Bottom)
Ionization Energy (IE) Increases (with subshell exceptions) Decreases
Electron Affinity (EA) Becomes more negative / increases Decreases / less exothermic
AFNS High-Yield Tip: Nitrogen ($\text{N}$) has a higher first ionization energy than Oxygen ($\text{O}$) because it has a stable, half-filled $2p^3$ subshell. Chlorine ($\text{Cl}$) has a higher electron affinity than Fluorine ($\text{F}$) because Fluorine's small size creates strong electron-electron repulsions that weaken incoming bonds.

Chapter 12: Electronegativity & Metallic Character

  • Electronegativity (EN): A relative measure of an atom's ability to attract the shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond. Commonly measured using the Pauling Scale.
  • Metallic Character: The relative ease with which an atom can lose electrons (also referred to as electropositivity).
  • EN Trends: Increases across a period and decreases down a group. Non-metals have high EN values, while metals have low EN values.
  • Metallic Trends: Decreases across a period as nuclear attraction tightens and increases down a group as valence electrons are held more loosely.
Element Identity Pauling EN Value Periodic Table Extremity Status
Fluorine ($\text{F}$) $4.0$ Most Electronegative / Strongest Non-metal
Cesium ($\text{Cs}$) $0.7$ Least Electronegative / Most Metallic Active Base
AFNS High-Yield Tip: If the electronegativity difference ($\Delta \text{EN}$) between two bonded atoms is greater than 1.7, the bond is primarily ionic. If it is less than 1.7, the bond is predominantly covalent.

Chapter 13: Atomic Radius & Ionic Radius Trends

  • Atomic Radius: Half the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms joined by a single covalent bond. It decreases across a period and increases down a group.
  • Cationic Radius: Cations are always smaller than their parent neutral atoms ($r_{\text{cation}} < r_{\text{atom}}$) due to the loss of electrons, which increases the effective nuclear charge pulling on the remaining electrons.
  • Anionic Radius: Anions are always larger than their parent neutral atoms ($r_{\text{anion}} > r_{\text{atom}}$) because adding electrons increases inter-electronic repulsion, expanding the electron cloud.
  • Isoelectronic Series: Atoms or ions that have the same number of electrons. For these, the ionic radius decreases as the atomic number (nuclear charge) increases.
Isoelectronic Ion species Proton Count ($Z$) Electron Count Relative Size Evaluation
$\text{O}^{2-}$ 8 protons 10 electrons Largest Ionic Radius (Weakest nuclear hold)
$\text{F}^{-}$ 9 protons 10 electrons Intermediate Radius Size
$\text{Na}^{+}$ 11 protons 10 electrons Smaller Radius Size
$\text{Mg}^{2+}$ 12 protons 10 electrons Smallest Ionic Radius (Strongest nuclear pull)
AFNS High-Yield Tip: When evaluating an isoelectronic series, remember: More protons equals a smaller size. A higher positive charge in the nucleus exerts a stronger pull on the same fixed number of surrounding electrons.
