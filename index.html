Discovery of Electron, Proton and Neutron

Topic Introduction

Every substance in the universe is made up of atoms. For a long time, scientists believed the atom was indivisible — the smallest possible particle. However, a series of brilliant experiments in the late 19th and early 20th centuries proved that atoms are themselves built from even smaller particles called subatomic particles. The three fundamental subatomic particles are the electron, the proton, and the neutron. Understanding how each was discovered is essential for FSC students and for competitive entry tests such as AFNS, NUMS, and nursing examinations.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Atom: The smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction and retains the chemical properties of that element.

Subatomic Particles: Particles smaller than an atom that make up its internal structure. The three main subatomic particles are the electron, proton, and neutron.

Electron: A negatively charged subatomic particle found outside the nucleus of an atom. Its charge is −1 and its mass is approximately 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg (negligible compared to proton and neutron).

Proton: A positively charged subatomic particle found inside the nucleus. Its charge is +1 and its mass is approximately 1.673 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (1 atomic mass unit, amu).

Neutron: A neutral (no charge) subatomic particle found inside the nucleus. Its mass is approximately 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, slightly greater than a proton.

Historical Background

In 1897, British physicist J.J. Thomson discovered the electron using cathode ray tubes. Ernest Rutherford identified the proton in 1919 through his gold foil and nitrogen bombardment experiments. The neutron remained elusive until 1932, when British physicist James Chadwick discovered it by bombarding beryllium with alpha particles. These three discoveries laid the foundation for modern atomic theory.

Main Concepts

1. Discovery of the Electron — Cathode Ray Tube Experiment (J.J. Thomson, 1897)

J.J. Thomson passed electric current through a sealed glass tube containing gas at very low pressure. He observed rays travelling from the negative electrode (cathode) to the positive electrode (anode). These rays were called cathode rays.

Key observations of cathode rays:

  • They travel in straight lines from cathode to anode.
  • They cast shadows of objects placed in their path, proving they travel in straight lines.
  • They are deflected towards the positive plate when passed through an electric field — proving they carry negative charge.
  • They are deflected by a magnetic field.
  • They produce fluorescence when they hit a fluorescent screen.
  • The nature of cathode rays does not depend on the material of the cathode or the type of gas used.
  • They cause ionisation of gases.

Thomson concluded that cathode rays consist of negatively charged particles, which he called electrons. He measured the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of the electron: e/m = 1.7588 × 10¹¹ C/kg.

Later, American physicist Robert Millikan determined the actual charge of the electron using his famous Oil Drop Experiment (1909): charge = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C. Using this and Thomson's e/m ratio, the mass of the electron was calculated as 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg.

2. Discovery of the Proton

When cathode rays were discovered, scientists also observed positively charged rays travelling from the anode towards the cathode. These were called canal rays (or positive rays or anode rays), first observed by Eugen Goldstein in 1886.

The canal rays had properties opposite to cathode rays:

  • They travel from anode to cathode.
  • They carry positive charge.
  • Their e/m ratio varies with the type of gas used (unlike cathode rays).
  • The lightest positive ray was obtained when hydrogen gas was used. This particle was named the proton.

In 1919, Ernest Rutherford confirmed the existence of the proton by bombarding nitrogen gas with alpha particles. The experiment produced hydrogen nuclei (protons), proving that the proton is a fundamental constituent of the nucleus.

3. Discovery of the Neutron (James Chadwick, 1932)

In 1932, James Chadwick discovered the neutron. He bombarded beryllium with alpha particles (helium nuclei) and observed that uncharged, highly penetrating radiation was emitted.

Reaction: ⁹Be + ⁴He → ¹²C + ¹n

Chadwick determined that this radiation consisted of neutral particles with a mass approximately equal to that of the proton. He called them neutrons.

Why was the neutron discovered last? Because it carries no charge, it is not deflected by electric or magnetic fields, making it very difficult to detect.

Important Features

  • Electrons are found in the space outside the nucleus (electron cloud / orbitals).
  • Protons and neutrons are found inside the nucleus (collectively called nucleons).
  • The number of protons = atomic number (Z) = defines the identity of the element.
  • The number of protons + number of neutrons = mass number (A).
  • In a neutral atom, number of protons = number of electrons.
  • Neutrons contribute to the mass of the nucleus but not to its charge.

Applications

  • Discovery of the electron led to the development of electronics, television, and X-ray technology.
  • Knowledge of proton structure is fundamental to nuclear physics and chemistry.
  • Discovery of the neutron led to the understanding of nuclear reactions, fission, and the development of nuclear energy.
  • Neutrons are used in nuclear reactors and neutron therapy for cancer treatment.

Bohr's Model of Atom

Topic Introduction

After Rutherford's nuclear model of the atom (1911), scientists faced a major problem: according to classical physics, an electron revolving around the nucleus should continuously lose energy and eventually spiral into the nucleus — but atoms are stable. In 1913, Danish physicist Niels Bohr proposed a new model of the atom that solved this problem and successfully explained the line spectrum of hydrogen. Bohr's model is one of the most important topics in atomic structure for FSC exams and competitive entry tests.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Bohr's Model: A model of the atom proposed by Niels Bohr in 1913, in which electrons revolve around the nucleus in fixed circular orbits (energy levels) without radiating energy.

Energy Level (Shell / Orbit): A fixed circular path around the nucleus at a specific distance and energy. Electrons can only exist in these allowed energy levels.

Ground State: The lowest energy level in which an electron normally resides.

Excited State: A higher energy level to which an electron jumps when it absorbs energy.

Line Spectrum: A spectrum consisting of discrete lines of specific wavelengths, produced when electrons fall from higher to lower energy levels.

Historical Background

Rutherford's nuclear model (1911) described the atom as a small, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by electrons. However, this model could not explain atomic stability or the line spectrum of hydrogen. Bohr built on Rutherford's model and incorporated Planck's quantum theory to produce his revolutionary atomic model in 1913, which won him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1922.

Main Concepts

Postulates of Bohr's Model

Bohr's model is based on four main postulates:

Postulate 1 — Fixed Orbits: Electrons revolve around the nucleus in fixed circular paths called orbits or shells or energy levels. As long as an electron stays in its orbit, it does not radiate energy.

Postulate 2 — Quantised Angular Momentum: An electron can only revolve in those orbits in which its angular momentum (mvr) is a whole number multiple of h/2π:

mvr = nh/2π

where m = mass of electron, v = velocity, r = radius, n = principal quantum number (1, 2, 3…), h = Planck's constant.

Postulate 3 — Energy Absorption and Emission: An electron can jump from a lower energy level to a higher energy level by absorbing a photon of energy equal to the difference between the two levels. It can fall back to a lower level by emitting a photon of the same energy:

ΔE = E₂ − E₁ = hν

where h = Planck's constant, ν = frequency of radiation emitted/absorbed.

Postulate 4 — Stability of Orbits: The electron does not lose energy while revolving in its fixed orbit. Only when it jumps between orbits does it gain or lose energy.

Bohr's Equation for Energy of an Orbit

The energy of an electron in the nth orbit of hydrogen is:

Eₙ = −13.6 / n² eV

The negative sign indicates the electron is bound to the nucleus (lower energy = more stable). As n increases, energy becomes less negative (electron moves away, more energy).

Bohr's Equation for Radius of an Orbit

rₙ = 0.529 × n² Å (for hydrogen)

The radius of the first Bohr orbit (n=1) = 0.529 Å = Bohr radius (a₀).

Explanation of Hydrogen Spectrum

When hydrogen gas is heated or electricity is passed through it, electrons jump to higher energy levels. When they fall back, they emit radiation of specific wavelengths, producing a line spectrum. The series of lines are named:

  • Lyman Series — electrons fall to n=1 (ultraviolet region)
  • Balmer Series — electrons fall to n=2 (visible region) — the most famous series
  • Paschen Series — electrons fall to n=3 (infrared region)
  • Brackett Series — electrons fall to n=4 (infrared)
  • Pfund Series — electrons fall to n=5 (infrared)

Important Features

  • Bohr's model works well for hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions (He⁺, Li²⁺).
  • It successfully explains the line spectrum of hydrogen.
  • It introduced the concept of quantised energy levels.
  • It uses the concept of angular momentum quantisation.

Defects (Limitations) of Bohr's Model

  • It fails to explain the spectra of multi-electron atoms (elements other than hydrogen).
  • It cannot explain the fine structure of spectral lines (splitting of lines under high resolution).
  • It fails to explain the Zeeman Effect (splitting of spectral lines in a magnetic field).
  • It fails to explain the Stark Effect (splitting of spectral lines in an electric field).
  • It treats the electron as a particle, not considering its wave nature (contradicts de Broglie's hypothesis).
  • It violates Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle by specifying exact positions and velocities of electrons.
  • It cannot explain chemical bonding or molecular formation.

Applications

  • Bohr's model explains the emission spectra of hydrogen — the basis of spectroscopy.
  • It provides the foundation for understanding atomic energy levels.
  • It explains why hydrogen emits coloured light when excited (used in spectral analysis).
  • It introduced quantum ideas into atomic physics, paving the way for quantum mechanics.

Quantum Theory

Topic Introduction

Classical physics could not explain certain phenomena related to light and energy at the atomic level — such as blackbody radiation and the photoelectric effect. In 1900, German physicist Max Planck proposed the revolutionary Quantum Theory, which stated that energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously but in discrete packets called quanta. This theory transformed our understanding of matter, light, and atoms, and gave birth to modern quantum mechanics.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Quantum Theory: The theory proposed by Max Planck (1900) stating that energy is quantised — it can only be emitted or absorbed in discrete packets called quanta (singular: quantum).

Quantum (Photon): The smallest discrete packet (bundle) of energy that can be emitted or absorbed by an atom. For light, a quantum is called a photon.

Planck's Constant (h): A fundamental physical constant: h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s.

Photoelectric Effect: The emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of sufficient frequency falls on it.

de Broglie's Hypothesis: The proposal by Louis de Broglie (1924) that all matter (not just light) has both particle and wave properties.

Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle: It is impossible to determine both the exact position and exact momentum (velocity) of an electron simultaneously.

Historical Background

By the late 19th century, two major phenomena could not be explained by classical physics:

  • Blackbody Radiation: Hot objects emit light of specific colours (wavelengths), and classical theory predicted infinite energy at high frequencies — the "ultraviolet catastrophe."
  • Photoelectric Effect: Metals emit electrons when light shines on them, but only if the light's frequency exceeds a minimum threshold — regardless of intensity.

Max Planck resolved blackbody radiation in 1900, and Albert Einstein applied quantum theory to explain the photoelectric effect in 1905, winning the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921.

Main Concepts

1. Planck's Quantum Theory (1900)

Max Planck proposed the following key ideas:

  • Energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously. It is emitted or absorbed in whole-number multiples of small packets called quanta.
  • The energy of one quantum is proportional to the frequency of the radiation:

E = hν

where E = energy of one quantum (joules), h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s), ν (nu) = frequency of radiation (Hz).

Since c = νλ, this can also be written as: E = hc/λ

where c = speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s), λ = wavelength.

  • Energy can only be emitted in amounts of hν, 2hν, 3hν, etc. — but never fractional amounts like 1.5hν.

2. Einstein and the Photoelectric Effect (1905)

Einstein extended Planck's idea and explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light itself is made up of quanta (now called photons). Each photon carries energy E = hν.

Key observations of the Photoelectric Effect:

  • Electrons are emitted only if the frequency of light ≥ threshold frequency (ν₀).
  • Increasing the intensity (brightness) of light increases the number of electrons emitted, not their energy.
  • Increasing the frequency of light increases the kinetic energy of emitted electrons.
  • Emission is instantaneous — no time delay.

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation: KE = hν − hν₀ = hν − W

where W = hν₀ = work function (minimum energy needed to remove an electron).

3. de Broglie's Wave-Particle Duality (1924)

French physicist Louis de Broglie proposed that if light (which was thought to be a wave) can behave as a particle (photon), then matter (which was thought to be a particle) should also have a wave nature.

de Broglie's equation: λ = h/mv = h/p

where λ = wavelength of the particle, m = mass, v = velocity, p = momentum.

This concept is called wave-particle duality. It was confirmed by electron diffraction experiments (Davisson and Germer, 1927).

4. Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle (1927)

German physicist Werner Heisenberg proposed that it is fundamentally impossible to measure both the exact position (Δx) and exact momentum (Δp) of an electron at the same time:

Δx · Δp ≥ h/4π

The more precisely we know the position of an electron, the less precisely we can know its momentum (velocity), and vice versa. This principle made Bohr's fixed circular orbits impossible and led to the concept of orbitals (probability regions).

5. Schrödinger's Wave Equation (1926)

Austrian physicist Erwin Schrödinger combined wave mechanics with quantum theory to produce the wave equation, which describes the behaviour of electrons in atoms. The solutions of this equation give orbitals — regions in space where there is a high probability of finding an electron. This forms the basis of the modern quantum mechanical model of the atom.

Important Features

  • Energy is quantised: it comes in discrete packets, not continuous amounts.
  • E = hν relates energy directly to frequency.
  • Higher frequency = higher energy (e.g., UV light has more energy per photon than visible light).
  • Quantum theory applies to atomic and subatomic systems.
  • Wave-particle duality applies to both light and matter.
  • Uncertainty principle rules out exact electron paths — orbitals replace orbits.

Applications

  • Explains blackbody radiation and the colour of hot objects.
  • Explains the photoelectric effect — basis of solar cells and photovoltaic devices.
  • Foundation of quantum mechanics, which explains atomic structure, chemical bonding, and spectroscopy.
  • Used in laser technology, semiconductors, and MRI machines.
  • Explains why atoms have specific energy levels and emit specific colours of light.

Quantum Numbers

Topic Introduction

In the quantum mechanical model of the atom, the behaviour and location of electrons cannot be described by simple circular orbits. Instead, each electron in an atom is described by a set of four numbers called quantum numbers. These numbers define the energy level, shape, orientation, and spin of an electron's orbital. Quantum numbers are essential for understanding electron configurations, the Periodic Table, and chemical properties of elements. This is a highly tested topic in AFNS, NUMS, and FSC examinations.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Quantum Numbers: A set of four numbers (n, l, m, s) that completely describe the state of an electron in an atom — its energy level, sublevel, orbital orientation, and spin direction.

Orbital: A three-dimensional region in space around the nucleus where there is a high probability (90–95%) of finding an electron.

Historical Background

Bohr introduced the concept of the principal quantum number (n). Later, as spectroscopic evidence revealed the fine structure of spectral lines, Arnold Sommerfeld introduced the azimuthal quantum number. The magnetic quantum number emerged to explain the Zeeman effect, and the spin quantum number was proposed by Uhlenbeck and Goudsmit in 1925 to account for the electron's intrinsic spin.

Main Concepts — The Four Quantum Numbers

Overview

Every electron in an atom is described by a unique set of four quantum numbers:

  • n = Principal Quantum Number
  • l = Azimuthal (Angular Momentum) Quantum Number
  • m (or ml) = Magnetic Quantum Number
  • s (or ms) = Spin Quantum Number

Important Features

  • No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of all four quantum numbers (Pauli Exclusion Principle).
  • Quantum numbers arise as solutions to the Schrödinger wave equation.
  • The first three quantum numbers (n, l, m) define the orbital; the fourth (s) defines the electron within that orbital.
  • Each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons (with opposite spins).

Applications

  • Used to write electron configurations of all elements.
  • Explain the shapes and orientations of orbitals (s, p, d, f).
  • Explain the spectral lines of atoms (each line corresponds to a specific quantum transition).
  • Form the basis for understanding chemical bonding and the Periodic Table.

Principal Quantum Number (n)

Topic Introduction

The principal quantum number, represented by the symbol n, is the most fundamental of the four quantum numbers. It was the first quantum number introduced, by Niels Bohr in 1913. It describes the main energy level (shell) of an electron and determines the size and overall energy of the orbital. A clear understanding of the principal quantum number is essential for writing electron configurations and solving problems in AFNS and FSC examinations.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Principal Quantum Number (n): A quantum number that specifies the main energy level (shell) of an electron in an atom. It determines the size of the orbital and the primary energy of the electron.

Shell: A main energy level designated by the principal quantum number n. Shells are also labelled with letters: K, L, M, N for n = 1, 2, 3, 4.

Historical Background

Niels Bohr introduced the concept of quantised energy levels in 1913. The principal quantum number n originated from Bohr's postulate that electrons can only exist in certain allowed orbits. It was later incorporated into quantum mechanics through Schrödinger's wave equation, where n appears as a natural solution parameter.

Main Concepts

Allowed Values of n

n can take any positive integer value: n = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7…

For all known elements in their ground states, n ranges from 1 to 7.

What n Determines

  • Energy of the electron: Higher n = higher (less negative) energy. Electrons with higher n are farther from the nucleus and less tightly bound.
  • Size of the orbital: Higher n = larger orbital (electron is farther from nucleus on average).
  • Shell label: n=1 is the K shell, n=2 is the L shell, n=3 is the M shell, n=4 is the N shell.
  • Number of subshells in a shell: A shell with principal quantum number n contains n subshells (l = 0, 1, 2, … n−1).
  • Number of orbitals in a shell: Total orbitals = n².
  • Maximum number of electrons in a shell: 2n².

Energy Formula (Hydrogen-like atoms)

Eₙ = −13.6 / n² eV (for hydrogen)

As n increases, the energy becomes less negative — the electron is less bound and higher in energy.

Important Features

  • n = 1 is the lowest energy level (ground state shell for most atoms).
  • Electrons in higher n shells are farther from the nucleus and easier to remove (lower ionisation energy contribution).
  • n determines the period an element belongs to in the Periodic Table: elements in period 3 have their outermost electrons in the n=3 shell.
  • The maximum number of electrons per shell: K(n=1)=2, L(n=2)=8, M(n=3)=18, N(n=4)=32.

Applications

  • Determines the energy shell an electron occupies.
  • Used in writing electron configurations (e.g., 2, 8, 8 distribution).
  • Relates to the period number in the Periodic Table.
  • Used to calculate orbital energies in hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions.

Azimuthal Quantum Number (l)

Topic Introduction

Within each main energy level (shell) defined by the principal quantum number n, electrons can exist in different subshells with different shapes. The azimuthal quantum number (also called the angular momentum quantum number or subsidiary quantum number), represented by l, defines the shape of the orbital and the sublevel (subshell) in which an electron resides. It explains the fine structure of spectral lines and is essential for understanding orbital geometry.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Azimuthal Quantum Number (l): A quantum number that defines the shape of an orbital and the sublevel (subshell) of an electron within a given main energy level (n). It also represents the angular momentum of the electron.

Subshell (Sublevel): A subdivision within a main energy shell, designated by the values of l. The subshells are named s, p, d, and f.

Angular Momentum: The rotational momentum of the electron as it moves around the nucleus, related to the shape of its orbital.

Historical Background

Arnold Sommerfeld extended Bohr's model in 1916 by proposing that electron orbits could be elliptical as well as circular. To account for this, he introduced the azimuthal quantum number l. The concept was later incorporated into quantum mechanics, where l describes orbital shapes as solutions to the Schrödinger equation.

Main Concepts

Allowed Values of l

For a given value of n, l can take integer values from 0 to (n − 1):

  • If n = 1: l = 0 only → one subshell (s)
  • If n = 2: l = 0, 1 → two subshells (s, p)
  • If n = 3: l = 0, 1, 2 → three subshells (s, p, d)
  • If n = 4: l = 0, 1, 2, 3 → four subshells (s, p, d, f)

Sublevel Names

Each value of l corresponds to a sublevel with a specific name:

  • l = 0 → s subshell (sharp) — spherical shape
  • l = 1 → p subshell (principal) — dumbbell shape
  • l = 2 → d subshell (diffuse) — cloverleaf shape
  • l = 3 → f subshell (fundamental) — complex shape

Angular Momentum Formula

The angular momentum of an electron is: L = √[l(l+1)] × h/2π

Number of Orbitals in a Subshell

Each subshell contains (2l + 1) orbitals:

  • s (l=0): 2(0)+1 = 1 orbital
  • p (l=1): 2(1)+1 = 3 orbitals
  • d (l=2): 2(2)+1 = 5 orbitals
  • f (l=3): 2(3)+1 = 7 orbitals

Maximum Electrons Per Subshell

Since each orbital holds 2 electrons, the maximum electrons per subshell = 2(2l+1):

  • s: 2 electrons
  • p: 6 electrons
  • d: 10 electrons
  • f: 14 electrons

Important Features

  • l determines the shape of the orbital, not its size (size depends on n).
  • For the same n, subshells have slightly different energies: s < p < d < f.
  • The s orbital is spherically symmetric (same in all directions).
  • The three p orbitals are identical in shape but oriented along x, y, and z axes.
  • d and f orbitals have more complex shapes and orientations.

Applications

  • Explains why spectral lines have fine structure.
  • Used to determine the number of subshells and orbitals in each energy level.
  • Identifies the type of bonding orbital (s, p, d) in chemical bonds.
  • Essential for writing complete electron configurations.

Magnetic Quantum Number (m or ml)

Topic Introduction

Orbitals of the same subshell (same l) can have different orientations in space. The magnetic quantum number, represented by m or ml, describes the orientation (direction) of an orbital in three-dimensional space relative to an external magnetic field. It explains why spectral lines split into multiple lines when an atom is placed in a magnetic field — a phenomenon known as the Zeeman Effect.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Magnetic Quantum Number (m): A quantum number that describes the orientation of an orbital in space relative to the three axes (x, y, z). It defines how many orbitals exist within a given subshell and their spatial directions.

Zeeman Effect: The splitting of spectral lines into multiple closely spaced lines when an atom is placed in an external magnetic field, explained by different orientations of orbitals (different m values).

Historical Background

In 1896, Dutch physicist Pieter Zeeman discovered that spectral lines split when atoms are placed in a magnetic field. To explain this, scientists introduced the magnetic quantum number m. In a magnetic field, orbitals of the same subshell but different m values have slightly different energies, causing the observed splitting.

Main Concepts

Allowed Values of m

For a given value of l, m can take integer values from −l to +l (including 0):

Number of m values = (2l + 1)

  • If l = 0 (s subshell): m = 0 only → 1 orbital
  • If l = 1 (p subshell): m = −1, 0, +1 → 3 orbitals (px, py, pz)
  • If l = 2 (d subshell): m = −2, −1, 0, +1, +2 → 5 orbitals
  • If l = 3 (f subshell): m = −3, −2, −1, 0, +1, +2, +3 → 7 orbitals

Physical Meaning

Each value of m corresponds to a specific orbital with a specific orientation in space:

  • The three p orbitals (m = −1, 0, +1) are oriented along the x, y, and z axes respectively.
  • In the absence of a magnetic field, all orbitals within a subshell have equal energy (degenerate).
  • In a magnetic field, orbitals of the same subshell but different m values have slightly different energies, causing the Zeeman Effect.

Important Features

  • m describes orientation of orbital, not its shape or energy (in absence of magnetic field).
  • In the absence of a magnetic field, all orbitals within a subshell are degenerate (same energy).
  • In the presence of a magnetic field, degeneracy is broken and orbitals have slightly different energies.
  • The total number of m values for a given l = (2l+1), which equals the number of orbitals in that subshell.

Applications

  • Explains the Zeeman Effect (splitting of spectral lines in a magnetic field).
  • Determines the number of orbitals in each subshell.
  • Used to assign orbital positions when drawing electron box diagrams.
  • Important in understanding MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) technology.

Spin Quantum Number (s or ms)

Topic Introduction

Even after specifying the energy level (n), shape (l), and orientation (m) of an orbital, a fourth property of the electron was needed to fully describe its state. This property is the intrinsic spin of the electron — an angular momentum inherent to the electron itself, like the Earth spinning on its own axis. The spin quantum number (ms) describes the direction of this spin. It can have only two values, meaning each orbital can hold at most two electrons with opposite spins.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Spin Quantum Number (ms): A quantum number that describes the intrinsic angular momentum (spin) of an electron. It can take only two values: +½ (spin up, ↑) or −½ (spin down, ↓).

Electron Spin: The intrinsic angular momentum of an electron, analogous to a particle spinning on its own axis. It is a quantum mechanical property with no classical equivalent.

Spin Up (↑): Electron spin in one direction, ms = +½.

Spin Down (↓): Electron spin in the opposite direction, ms = −½.

Historical Background

In 1925, Dutch physicists George Uhlenbeck and Samuel Goudsmit proposed the concept of electron spin to explain the fine structure of spectral lines and certain magnetic properties of atoms that could not be explained by the first three quantum numbers. The spin quantum number was later confirmed by the Stern-Gerlach experiment (1922), in which a beam of silver atoms was split into two distinct beams when passed through an inhomogeneous magnetic field — demonstrating two possible spin states.

Main Concepts

Allowed Values of ms

The spin quantum number has only two possible values:

  • ms = +½ → spin up (↑) — clockwise spin
  • ms = −½ → spin down (↓) — anticlockwise spin

These are the only allowed values — there is no ms = 0 or any other value.

Significance of the Spin Quantum Number

  • Since each orbital is defined by a specific set of n, l, m values, and ms can be +½ or −½, each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons.
  • The two electrons in the same orbital must have opposite spins — one +½ and one −½.
  • This is directly related to the Pauli Exclusion Principle.

Paired and Unpaired Electrons

Paired electrons: Two electrons in the same orbital with opposite spins (↑↓). Their magnetic effects cancel out.

Unpaired electron: An electron alone in an orbital (↑ or ↓). Contributes to the magnetic properties of an atom.

Paramagnetic: Atoms/ions with unpaired electrons are attracted to a magnetic field (e.g., O₂, Fe).

Diamagnetic: Atoms/ions with all electrons paired are repelled by a magnetic field (e.g., Ne, Zn).

Important Features

  • ms has only two values: +½ and −½.
  • Spin is an intrinsic property of the electron — it does not mean the electron physically rotates.
  • The spin quantum number is independent of n, l, and m.
  • Electrons with the same spin in different orbitals have lower energy (basis of Hund's Rule).

Applications

  • Explains the two-electron capacity of each orbital.
  • Foundation of the Pauli Exclusion Principle.
  • Explains paramagnetism and diamagnetism of atoms and molecules.
  • Basis of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) and MRI technology.
  • Explains the fine structure of spectral lines.

Pauli Exclusion Principle

Topic Introduction

When filling electrons into orbitals, a fundamental rule governs how electrons are distributed: the Pauli Exclusion Principle. This principle, proposed by Austrian physicist Wolfgang Pauli in 1925, sets a strict limit on how many electrons can occupy a single orbital and explains why each orbital can hold at most two electrons. It is the cornerstone of electron configuration rules and is essential for understanding atomic structure.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of all four quantum numbers (n, l, m, ms). Equivalently, an orbital can contain at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spins.

Historical Background

Wolfgang Pauli proposed this principle in 1925 to explain the structure of electron shells and the arrangement of elements in the Periodic Table. Pauli received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1945 for this discovery. The principle was derived from quantum mechanics and is a fundamental law of nature that applies to all fermions (particles with half-integer spin, like electrons).

Main Concepts

Statement of the Principle

"No two electrons in an atom can have the same values for all four quantum numbers (n, l, m, ms)."

Since n, l, and m define a specific orbital, and ms can only be +½ or −½, a maximum of two electrons can occupy any single orbital — and they must have opposite spins (+½ and −½).

Understanding the Principle Through Quantum Numbers

Consider two electrons in the 1s orbital of helium (He):

  • Electron 1: n=1, l=0, m=0, ms=+½ (↑)
  • Electron 2: n=1, l=0, m=0, ms=−½ (↓)

The first three quantum numbers are the same (same orbital) but ms is different — so this is allowed. A third electron would require ms=+½ or −½, but both are taken — so the 1s orbital is full. This is why helium's 1s orbital can hold only 2 electrons.

Consequences of the Pauli Exclusion Principle

  • Each orbital (s, p, d, f) can hold a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • The maximum capacities of subshells are: s=2, p=6, d=10, f=14.
  • The maximum electrons per shell = 2n².
  • It explains why electrons fill successive energy levels rather than all collapsing into the lowest level.
  • It explains the distinct shells and subshells in the Periodic Table.
  • It is the reason atoms have the electron configurations they do.

Pauli Principle and the Periodic Table

Without the Pauli Exclusion Principle, all electrons would crowd into the 1s orbital, all atoms would have the same chemistry, and the Periodic Table as we know it would not exist. The principle is therefore directly responsible for the diversity of elements and their chemical properties.

Important Features

  • The Pauli Exclusion Principle applies to all fermions (electrons, protons, neutrons) — particles with half-integer spin.
  • It does NOT apply to bosons (particles with integer spin, like photons).
  • Pauli himself described it as "an exclusion of states" — states are either occupied or unoccupied, never over-occupied.

Applications

  • Determines the maximum number of electrons in each orbital, subshell, and shell.
  • Foundation of electron configuration and the Aufbau principle.
  • Explains the stability and diversity of chemical elements.
  • Used in the design of electronic devices (semiconductors, transistors).
  • Important in nuclear physics and astrophysics (neutron stars are supported by Pauli exclusion among neutrons).

Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity

Topic Introduction

When electrons fill degenerate orbitals (orbitals of equal energy within the same subshell), there is a question: should they pair up in the same orbital, or fill separate orbitals first? The answer is given by Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity, proposed by German physicist Friedrich Hund. This rule explains how electrons occupy p, d, and f orbitals and is essential for correctly drawing electron configurations, orbital box diagrams, and predicting the magnetic properties of atoms.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Hund's Rule: When electrons are distributed among orbitals of equal energy (degenerate orbitals) in the same subshell, they fill each orbital singly first (with the same spin direction), before any pairing begins. This arrangement gives the atom maximum stability.

Degenerate Orbitals: Orbitals within the same subshell that have the same energy. For example, the three 2p orbitals (2px, 2py, 2pz) are degenerate.

Maximum Multiplicity: The state in which electrons in degenerate orbitals have the maximum number of parallel (same direction) spins.

Historical Background

Friedrich Hund developed this rule empirically in 1925 by analysing atomic spectra. He noticed that atoms were most stable when electrons spread out among degenerate orbitals with parallel spins rather than pairing up in a single orbital. This was later explained quantum mechanically: electrons with parallel spins tend to avoid each other more effectively (exchange energy), reducing electron-electron repulsion and thus lowering the total energy of the atom.

Main Concepts

Statement of Hund's Rule

"Electrons in degenerate orbitals (same energy, same subshell) occupy each orbital singly with parallel spins before any orbital is doubly occupied."

Three Key Points of Hund's Rule

  • Point 1 — One electron per orbital first: When filling a subshell, place one electron in each orbital before pairing any electrons.
  • Point 2 — Parallel spins: All singly occupied orbitals have electrons with the same spin direction (usually shown as all ↑ or all ↓).
  • Point 3 — Maximum stability: The arrangement with maximum unpaired electrons with parallel spins has the lowest energy (most stable).

Reason for Hund's Rule

  • Electrons carry negative charges and repel each other.
  • Spreading electrons across separate orbitals minimises electron-electron repulsion.
  • Electrons with parallel spins (↑↑) avoid being in the same region of space more than antiparallel electrons (↑↓), due to the Pauli Exclusion Principle — this is called the exchange energy effect.
  • The lower repulsion means lower energy = greater stability.

Examples

Carbon (C) — Z = 6, electron configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p²

The two 2p electrons: Correct → ↑_ ↑_ __ (one in 2px, one in 2py, both spin up). Wrong → ↑↓ __ __ (both paired in one orbital — violates Hund's Rule).

Nitrogen (N) — Z = 7, electron configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p³

Three 2p electrons: ↑_ ↑_ ↑_ (one in each 2p orbital, all spin up) — this is the correct arrangement.

Oxygen (O) — Z = 8, electron configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p⁴

Four 2p electrons: ↑↓ ↑_ ↑_ (first orbital pairs up, then one in each remaining — Hund's Rule is still followed during filling).

Important Features

  • Hund's Rule applies specifically to degenerate orbitals within the same subshell.
  • It works in conjunction with the Aufbau principle and Pauli Exclusion Principle.
  • Atoms following Hund's Rule correctly have maximum spin multiplicity in their ground state.
  • Violation of Hund's Rule always gives a higher-energy (less stable) arrangement.

Applications

  • Used to correctly draw orbital (box) diagrams for all elements.
  • Predicts the magnetic properties of atoms: atoms with unpaired electrons (paramagnetic) vs. all paired (diamagnetic).
  • Essential for understanding transition metal chemistry and their coloured compounds.
  • Explains why oxygen (O₂) is paramagnetic (has 2 unpaired electrons).

Mendeleev's Periodic Table

Topic Introduction

As the number of known elements grew in the 19th century, chemists searched for a logical way to organise them. Several scientists attempted to classify elements, but it was Russian chemist Dmitri Ivanovich Mendeleev who, in 1869, proposed the most successful and enduring periodic table, based on his observation that the properties of elements repeat periodically when arranged by increasing atomic mass. His system was so powerful that he used it to predict the existence of undiscovered elements — and was later proven right.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Periodic Table: A tabular arrangement of elements in rows (periods) and columns (groups) based on their atomic number and repeating chemical properties.

Mendeleev's Periodic Law: The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic masses. (Note: Modern law uses atomic number.)

Period: A horizontal row in the Periodic Table. Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells.

Group: A vertical column in the Periodic Table. Elements in the same group have similar chemical properties.

Historical Background

Before Mendeleev, several scientists attempted to classify elements:

  • Döbereiner's Triads (1829): Noticed that certain groups of three elements (triads) had properties where the middle element was the average of the other two (e.g., Li, Na, K). This was the first attempt at classification but limited.
  • Newlands' Law of Octaves (1865): John Newlands arranged elements by atomic mass and noticed that every eighth element had similar properties (like musical octaves). This worked only for lighter elements and was not widely accepted.
  • Lothar Meyer (1870): Independently proposed a periodic arrangement similar to Mendeleev's, but Mendeleev gets credit because he published first (1869) and made successful predictions.

Mendeleev published his periodic table in 1869 in the journal of the Russian Chemical Society.

Main Concepts

Mendeleev's Periodic Law

"The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic masses."

Structure of Mendeleev's Table

  • Elements were arranged in order of increasing atomic mass.
  • Elements with similar properties were placed in the same vertical column (group).
  • The table had 8 groups and 7 periods.
  • Groups were numbered I to VIII.

Achievements of Mendeleev's Periodic Table

  • Systematic arrangement: For the first time, all known elements were arranged in a logical, systematic manner.
  • Prediction of undiscovered elements: Mendeleev left gaps in his table and successfully predicted the properties of three yet-undiscovered elements:
    • Eka-boron → later discovered as Scandium (Sc, 1879)
    • Eka-aluminium → later discovered as Gallium (Ga, 1875)
    • Eka-silicon → later discovered as Germanium (Ge, 1886)
  • Correction of atomic masses: Where the properties of an element didn't fit, Mendeleev corrected the atomic mass (e.g., beryllium was corrected from 13.5 to 9).
  • Provided a framework that guided future chemical research.

Defects (Limitations) of Mendeleev's Periodic Table

  • Position of Hydrogen: Hydrogen could not be placed satisfactorily — it resembles both alkali metals (Group I) and halogens (Group VII).
  • Anomalous pairs: Certain pairs of elements had to be placed out of order of atomic masses to fit chemical properties: Co(58.9) before Ni(58.6); Te(127.6) before I(126.9); Ar(39.9) before K(39.1). (This was later resolved when atomic number replaced atomic mass.)
  • No place for isotopes: Isotopes of the same element have different atomic masses, so they would need separate places, but Mendeleev's table could not accommodate them.
  • Position of Lanthanides and Actinides: No proper position for these rare earth elements.
  • Noble gases not included: Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, etc.) were not yet discovered when Mendeleev made his table.
  • Dissimilar elements in the same group: Some groups contained chemically dissimilar elements (e.g., copper and alkali metals in Group I).
  • No explanation for periodicity: Mendeleev's table described periodicity but did not explain why it occurs.

Important Features

  • Based on atomic mass (not atomic number).
  • Had 63 elements when first published.
  • Left deliberate gaps for undiscovered elements — a revolutionary idea.
  • Predicted properties of missing elements remarkably accurately.

Applications

  • Provided the first logical classification of all known elements.
  • Allowed prediction of new elements and guided their discovery.
  • Inspired the development of the modern Periodic Table.
  • Still used as a basis for teaching the history and development of chemistry.

Modern Periodic Law and the Modern Periodic Table

Topic Introduction

Mendeleev's Periodic Table was a landmark achievement, but it had several limitations — especially the anomalous placement of certain pairs of elements. These problems were resolved in 1913 when British physicist Henry Moseley discovered that the fundamental property of an element is its atomic number (number of protons), not its atomic mass. This led to the Modern Periodic Law and the development of the Modern Long-Form Periodic Table, which is the table used today.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Modern Periodic Law: The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic numbers.

Atomic Number (Z): The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It uniquely identifies each element.

Modern Periodic Table: A tabular arrangement of all known elements in order of increasing atomic number, organised into periods (rows) and groups (columns), with elements showing periodic repetition of properties.

Historical Background

In 1913, Henry Moseley used X-ray spectroscopy to study the characteristic X-rays emitted by different elements. He discovered that the frequency of X-rays emitted was directly related to the atomic number (Z) of the element, not its atomic mass. This allowed elements to be arranged by atomic number, resolving all the anomalous pairs in Mendeleev's table and placing the Periodic Law on a firm theoretical foundation.

Main Concepts

Moseley's Law

Moseley found: √ν = a(Z − b)

where ν = frequency of X-rays, Z = atomic number, a and b are constants. This showed atomic number was the true fundamental property of elements.

Modern Periodic Law (Statement)

"The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic numbers."

Structure of the Modern Periodic Table

  • 118 elements are currently known and placed in the table.
  • The table has 7 periods (horizontal rows) and 18 groups (vertical columns).
  • Periods are numbered 1 to 7. The period number equals the number of electron shells (principal quantum number n) in the outermost occupied shell of those elements.
  • Groups are numbered 1 to 18 (IUPAC notation) or labelled IA–VIIA and IB–VIIB (older notation).

Blocks of the Periodic Table

Elements are divided into four blocks based on the subshell in which the last electron is added:

  • s-block: Groups 1–2 (and He). Last electron enters s orbital. Includes alkali metals (Group 1) and alkaline earth metals (Group 2). Highly reactive metals.
  • p-block: Groups 13–18. Last electron enters p orbital. Includes metalloids, non-metals, and noble gases. Most diverse block.
  • d-block: Groups 3–12. Last electron enters d orbital. Includes transition metals. Have variable oxidation states, coloured compounds, good conductors.
  • f-block: Lanthanides (Period 6) and Actinides (Period 7). Last electron enters f orbital. Placed at the bottom of the table separately. Lanthanides are rare earth metals; actinides include radioactive elements (uranium, thorium, etc.).

Periods of the Modern Periodic Table

  • Period 1: 2 elements (H, He) — only 1s subshell being filled.
  • Period 2: 8 elements — 2s and 2p subshells.
  • Period 3: 8 elements — 3s and 3p subshells.
  • Period 4: 18 elements — 4s, 3d, and 4p subshells.
  • Period 5: 18 elements — 5s, 4d, and 5p subshells.
  • Period 6: 32 elements — 6s, 4f, 5d, and 6p subshells.
  • Period 7: 32 elements (incomplete) — 7s, 5f, 6d, and 7p subshells.

Advantages of the Modern Periodic Table over Mendeleev's

  • All anomalous pairs (Co/Ni, Ar/K, Te/I) are now correctly placed by atomic number.
  • Isotopes of the same element have the same atomic number and occupy the same position — no conflict.
  • Noble gases (Group 18) have a definite place.
  • Lanthanides and actinides are accommodated (f-block, shown separately).
  • The table reflects the electronic configuration of elements directly.
  • Based on the fundamental property (atomic number), not a derived one (atomic mass).

Important Features

  • Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons → similar chemical properties.
  • Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells → properties change systematically across the period.
  • The 18-group table is called the Long-Form Periodic Table.
  • Transition metals (d-block) are placed between s-block and p-block.

Applications

  • Predicts the properties of elements based on their position.
  • Helps understand trends in atomic radius, ionisation energy, electronegativity, etc.
  • Used in chemistry education worldwide as the primary reference tool.
  • Guides the search for and naming of new elements (beyond element 118).

  • s-block: Groups 1–2; p-block: Groups 13–18; d-block: Groups 3–12; f-block: below the table.
  • Period number = number of electron shells in outermost occupied level.
  • Group number = number of valence electrons (for s and p blocks).
  • All anomalous pairs resolved by using atomic number.
  • Noble gases added as Group 18 (discovered after Mendeleev).
  • f-block contains lanthanides (57–71) and actinides (89–103).
  • Moseley's Law: √ν = a(Z − b) — linked X-ray frequency to atomic number.
  • AFNS Tip: The most frequently tested fact: Modern Periodic Law is based on atomic number, not atomic mass.

Atomic Radius

Topic Introduction

The atomic radius is a measure of the size of an atom. It is one of the fundamental periodic properties of elements — it changes predictably as you move across a period or down a group in the Periodic Table. Understanding atomic radius trends is essential for explaining many other periodic properties such as ionisation energy, electronegativity, and reactivity. It is frequently tested in AFNS, FSC, NUMS, and nursing entry examinations.

Complete Notes

Definitions

Atomic Radius: The distance from the centre of the nucleus to the outermost shell of electrons in an isolated atom. It is a measure of the size of an atom. Its unit is the picometer (pm) or Ångström (Å) (1 Å = 100 pm).

Covalent Radius: Half the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms bonded together by a covalent bond. Used for non-metals.

Metallic (Crystal) Radius: Half the distance between the nuclei of two adjacent atoms in a metallic crystal. Used for metals.

Van der Waals Radius: Half the distance between the nuclei of two identical atoms that are in contact but not bonded (just touching). Largest of all radius types.

Ionic Radius: The radius of an ion (charged atom). Cations are smaller than their parent atoms; anions are larger.

Shielding Effect (Screening Effect): The reduction in the effective nuclear charge experienced by outer electrons due to repulsion from inner electrons between them and the nucleus.

Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff): The net positive charge experienced by an electron after accounting for shielding by inner electrons. Zeff = Z − σ (where σ = shielding constant).

Historical Background

The concept of atomic size became important as the Periodic Table developed. Scientists observed that atomic properties vary systematically with atomic number. The measurement and comparison of atomic radii became possible through X-ray crystallography and spectroscopic techniques in the early 20th century.

Main Concepts

Why Atomic Radius Cannot Be Measured Directly

An atom has no definite boundary — the electron cloud extends theoretically to infinity, though the probability of finding an electron decreases rapidly with distance. Therefore, atomic radius is always measured indirectly, based on the distance between nuclei of two bonded or adjacent atoms.

Factors Affecting Atomic Radius

Factor 1 — Nuclear Charge (Z): Higher nuclear charge pulls electrons closer to the nucleus, decreasing atomic radius.

Factor 2 — Number of Electron Shells (n): More electron shells means electrons are farther from the nucleus, increasing atomic radius.

Factor 3 — Shielding Effect: Inner shell electrons shield outer electrons from the full nuclear charge. Greater shielding → less effective nuclear charge → outer electrons held less tightly → larger atomic radius.

Factor 4 — Penetration of Orbitals: Electrons in s orbitals penetrate closer to the nucleus and experience greater nuclear charge, making them held more tightly (smaller radius contribution).

Periodic Trends in Atomic Radius

Trend 1 — Across a Period (Left to Right):

  • Atomic radius decreases from left to right across a period.
  • Reason: As we move across a period, atomic number (Z) increases by 1 each time, but electrons are added to the same shell (same n). The shielding effect remains nearly constant (inner shells unchanged). Therefore, effective nuclear charge (Zeff) increases, pulling electrons closer and decreasing the radius.
  • Example in Period 2: Li (152 pm) > Be (112 pm) > B (87 pm) > C (77 pm) > N (75 pm) > O (73 pm) > F (64 pm) > Ne (58 pm).

Trend 2 — Down a Group (Top to Bottom):

  • Atomic radius increases from top to bottom down a group.
  • Reason: As we go down a group, new electron shells are added (n increases). Each new shell is farther from the nucleus. Also, the shielding effect increases with more inner shell electrons, reducing effective nuclear charge on the outer electrons. Both effects increase atomic size.
  • Example in Group 1: Li (152 pm) < Na (186 pm) < K (227 pm) < Rb (248 pm) < Cs (265 pm).

Atomic Radius of Noble Gases

Noble gases are generally given van der Waals radii (since they do not form bonds). Their van der Waals radii appear large, but their covalent or "true" atomic radii would actually be small. In comparisons across a period, noble gases appear to break the trend due to this difference in measurement type.

Ionic Radius vs Atomic Radius

  • Cation (positive ion): Formed by loss of electrons. Has fewer electrons but same nuclear charge → stronger pull on remaining electrons → smaller than parent atom. Example: Na (186 pm) → Na⁺ (102 pm).
  • Anion (negative ion): Formed by gain of electrons. Has more electrons with same nuclear charge → weaker pull per electron → larger than parent atom. Example: Cl (99 pm) → Cl⁻ (181 pm).
  • For isoelectronic species (same number of electrons): higher nuclear charge → smaller radius. Example: O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ (all have 10 electrons, but increasing Z).

Important Features

  • Atomic radius decreases across a period (left to right) due to increasing Z with same shielding.
  • Atomic radius increases down a group due to addition of new electron shells.
  • The smallest atom (by covalent radius) is hydrogen or helium (depending on comparison type); the largest is caesium (Cs).
  • Fluorine has the smallest atomic radius among non-noble-gas elements in Period 2.
  • Cations are always smaller than their parent neutral atoms.
  • Anions are always larger than their parent neutral atoms.

Applications

  • Explains reactivity trends: smaller atoms tend to have higher ionisation energies and electronegativities.
  • Explains the types of bonds formed: atoms of similar size tend to form covalent bonds.
  • Understanding ionic radii helps predict lattice energies and solubilities of ionic compounds.
  • Atomic radius trends underpin the explanation of all other periodic properties.

Quick Revision Notes

  • Atomic radius = distance from nucleus to outermost electrons. Unit: pm or Å.
  • Cannot be measured directly — always half the internuclear distance in bonded or adjacent atoms.
  • Types: covalent radius, metallic radius, van der Waals radius, ionic radius.
  • Across a period (→): atomic radius decreases — Z increases, same shielding, Zeff increases.
  • Down a group (↓): atomic radius increases — new shells added, more shielding.
  • Cation smaller than parent atom; anion larger than parent atom.
  • Isoelectronic series: higher Z → smaller radius (e.g., O²⁻ > F⁻ > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺).
  • Largest neutral atom: Caesium (Cs).
  • Smallest atom (Period 2, non-noble-gas): Fluorine (F).
  • Shielding effect: inner electrons reduce effective nuclear charge on outer electrons.
  • Zeff = Z − σ (effective nuclear charge = actual nuclear charge minus shielding constant).
  • Memory Aid: "Atomic radius goes Left-Large, Right-Small across a period; Top-Small, Bottom-Big down a group."
  • AFNS Tip: Trend questions are very common — know that radius decreases left to right and increases top to bottom. Also know cation < atom < anion.
